## Manuscript summary

The thesis argues that predictive multiplicity should be studied not only globally, but locally in feature space. It constructs finite top-$K$ Rashomon approximations from five model families, computes observation-wise predictive variance, builds symmetrized $k$NN graphs on preprocessed features, and uses Moran's $I$ and LISA to detect High--High multiplicity hotspots. The empirical story is coherent: COMPAS shows the clearest localized structure, German Credit has larger aggregate disagreement but weaker spatial concentration and very few HH points, Adult has broader/diffuse structure with many HH observations, and synthetic datasets validate recovery of known ambiguous regions.

Compared with an earlier draft structure, this version is materially stronger. The merged **Framework and Experimental Design** chapter is a clear improvement: the top-$K$ Rashomon approximation, split protocols, graph construction, null model, attribution caveats, and method-to-result mapping are now in one place. The appendix is also substantial and well used. The main remaining work is not "add more experiments," but **tighten claim calibration at the front of the thesis**, add a few small auditability tables, and sharpen positioning relative to very close recent work—especially on calibration.

## Overall assessment

This is a promising, mostly coherent empirical master's thesis with a distinctive spatial framing. Strengths include: the soft probability-space focus, the distinction between disagreement magnitude and spatial concentration, the fixed-test stability protocol, synthetic validation, descriptive attribution with explicit caveats in Chapter 3, and a thoughtful limitations section. The results chapter is evidence-rich and generally well interpreted.

The highest-risk remaining issues are:

1. The **abstract and summary still overstate certainty** ("confirm," "non-random") and do not yet state the finite top-$K$ operational Rashomon definition.
2. The **"near-optimal" claim is still not numerically audited** (no validation Brier spread for selected top-$K$ models).
3. **Calibration robustness** is empirically useful but under-positioned relative to recent work that treats calibration as multiplicity mitigation.
4. Some **hyperparameter language still sounds causal** ("explains," "drivers") despite the descriptive framework.
5. **Submission polish** items remain: dataset-size table, rule simplification, naming consistency, electronic appendix detail, and front-matter completeness.

I would not recommend many new experiments now. The best payoff is front-matter precision, two small tables, and selective wording fixes.

## Highest-priority issues

### high-1: Calibrate the abstract and early claims to match the actual methodological object

**Where:** Abstract (`thesis.tex`, lines 105--112); Results summary (`results.tex`, Section 4.8, item 1); Robustness intro (`results.tex`, line 163).

**Evidence:** The abstract says experiments "confirm that predictive multiplicity is consistently spatially structured and non-random," and refers to "a Rashomon set of near-optimal models" without stating that the empirical set is a finite top-$K$ rank-based approximation. Chapter 3.2 defines this clearly, but a reader who stops at the abstract may assume a tolerance-defined Rashomon set.

**Why it matters:** This is the first credibility check for a statistics examiner. The thesis has already fixed the methodology chapter; the abstract and summary have not yet caught up.

**Concrete fix:** In the abstract, replace:

> "confirm that predictive multiplicity is consistently spatially structured and non-random"

with:

> "show that predictive multiplicity is spatially structured under the tested benchmark settings and the prediction-permutation null"

and add one phrase such as:

> "across finite top-$K$ Rashomon approximations selected by validation Brier score."

Mirror the same wording in Results summary item 1.

### high-2: Add a small validation-Brier-gap table to defend "near-optimal"

**Where:** Section 3.2 (`framework_experimental_design.tex`); optionally Results 4.1.

**Evidence:** The thesis justifies top-$K$ selection well conceptually, but never reports how close the 25th-ranked model is to the best model in validation Brier score. The repository already stores `val_brier` in model metadata (`analysis/run_analysis.py`, selection by `meta["val_brier"]`).

**Why it matters:** Without this, "near-optimal" remains verbal. A skeptical reader can ask whether top-$K$ models span a wide absolute loss range, especially across families and datasets.

**Concrete fix:** Export and report, per dataset and run:

| Dataset | mean max gap | mean median gap | 95th percentile gap |

where gap = `val_brier - min(val_brier)` within the selected top-$K$ set. One compact table or three sentences would suffice.

### high-3: Position calibration robustness against recent calibration-multiplicity work

**Where:** Related Work 2.4 or 2.5; Results 4.4.3; Discussion 5.4.

**Evidence:** Section 4.4.3 uses Platt and isotonic calibration as a robustness check and finds that spatial autocorrelation persists. The thesis cites Cavus et al. on hyperparameters (`cavus_role_2025`) but not Cavus's 2026 work on calibration and multiplicity.

**CITATION TO VERIFY:** Mustafa Cavus, "Mitigating the Multiplicity Burden: The Role of Calibration in Reducing Predictive Multiplicity of Classifiers", arXiv:2603.11750 (v2, Mar 2026). That paper argues calibration can reduce predictive multiplicity globally; your result that Moran's $I$ remains positive is compatible but needs explicit contrast.

**Why it matters:** Otherwise Section 4.4.3 reads like an isolated internal check rather than a contribution-aware robustness analysis.

**Concrete fix:** Add one paragraph:

> Recent work studies calibration as a way to reduce predictive multiplicity globally. Our calibration analysis has a different role: it tests whether spatial hotspot conclusions are artifacts of probability scaling rather than proposing calibration as a mitigation strategy.

Then note explicitly that your finding (spatial structure survives calibration) complements, rather than contradicts, work showing calibration can lower aggregate disagreement.

### high-4: Replace remaining causal hyperparameter wording in Results

**Where:** Results 4.5.3 (`results.tex`, lines 431--432); Results summary item 5; Discussion 5.2 RQ4.

**Evidence:** Chapter 3.7 correctly says attribution is descriptive. But Results 4.5.3 still says regularization strength $C$ "explains nearly all within-family variation," and the summary calls hyperparameters "drivers."

**Why it matters:** Hyperparameters are sampled jointly and filtered by top-$K$ performance; grouped tertile decomposition is associative, not causal. The framework already knows this—the results chapter should match.

**Concrete fix:** Change, for example:

> "$C$ explains nearly all within-family variation"

to:

> "variation across grouped values of $C$ accounts for most of the observed variation in $V_m$ within retained logistic-regression models."

Keep "driver" only with an explicit descriptor such as "descriptive driver" or "associated source."

### high-5: Add a dataset characteristics table with sample sizes and test-set context

**Where:** Section 3.2, after dataset descriptions (`framework_experimental_design.tex`, lines 54--77).

**Evidence:** The thesis discusses HH counts and notes Adult's larger test set indirectly, but never gives a compact table with post-preprocessing sample sizes, test-set size, positive rate, and transformed feature dimension. German Credit's 200 test observations and ~5 HH points per run are central to interpreting weak hotspot-level claims.

**Why it matters:** Many cross-dataset comparisons (HH count, HH rate, stability, attribution inside HH) are hard to audit without this context.

**Concrete fix:** Add a table:

| Dataset | $n_{\text{train}}$ | $n_{\text{val}}$ | $n_{\text{test}}$ | positive rate | transformed dim |

This can be exported from the pipeline logs or preprocessing metadata.
