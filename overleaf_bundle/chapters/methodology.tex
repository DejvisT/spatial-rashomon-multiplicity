This chapter presents the methodology for analyzing predictive multiplicity through a
spatial lens. The framework is formulated independently of any particular dataset or
experimental configuration; its concrete instantiation is described in Chapter~4.

The methodological flow is as follows. We first construct a Rashomon set of near-optimal
models and compute observation-wise multiplicity metrics (predictive variance and conflict
ratio). We then build a feature-space graph to encode neighborhood relationships, quantify
global and local spatial clustering via Moran's $I$ and LISA, extract hotspot regions (HH
clusters) and their connected components, and finally compare the observed structure
against a permutation null. The resulting pipeline identifies \emph{where} in feature space
predictive instability concentrates and whether that concentration exceeds what would be
expected by chance.

\subsection{Problem Setting and Notation}

Let $(X, y)$ denote a supervised learning dataset with $N$ observations, where
$X \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$ is the feature matrix and
$y \in \{0,1\}^N$ is a binary target variable. Let $\mathcal{F}$ denote a hypothesis class
of predictive models, potentially spanning multiple model families and hyperparameter
configurations.

We consider probabilistic classifiers $f \in \mathcal{F}$ that output predicted probabilities
$\hat{p}_{fi} \in [0,1]$ for each observation $i \in \{1,\dots,N\}$. Model performance is
measured by a loss function $L(f)$ evaluated on held-out data. Throughout this chapter,
the framework is formulated for binary probabilistic classification; extensions to other
prediction settings are discussed later in the thesis.

\subsection{Rashomon Set Construction}
\label{sec:meth-rashomon}

Predictive multiplicity is studied through the Rashomon set, defined as the collection of
models whose performance lies within a tolerance $\varepsilon > 0$ of the best achievable
loss. In our setting, $\mathcal{F}$ is a \emph{finite} candidate pool: a set of models trained
with varying families and hyperparameter configurations on a common training set. Let
\[
L^* = \min_{f \in \mathcal{F}} L(f)
\]
denote the minimum validation loss among all candidates. The Rashomon set is then
\[
\mathcal{R}_\varepsilon = \{ f \in \mathcal{F} : L(f) \leq L^* + \varepsilon \}.
\]

In the empirical setting of this thesis, model quality is evaluated using the Brier score on
the validation set. This choice is aligned with the subsequent multiplicity analysis because
the primary response variable is the spread of predicted probabilities rather than only
thresholded labels.

The tolerance $\varepsilon$ controls the strictness of selection. Because the value of
$\varepsilon$ required to produce a given set size varies across datasets and splits, we use a practical \textbf{top-$K$ selection rule}: the $K$ models with the lowest validation
Brier score are selected, yielding $|\mathcal{R}| = K$. This provides a reproducible,
rank-based approximation to the Rashomon set while fixing its size across runs and
datasets. Selection can be \emph{global} (top-$K$ across all families) or \emph{per-family}
(top-$K$ within each model family); the latter is used when attributing multiplicity to
hyperparameters in Chapter~4.

\subsection{Observation-Wise Multiplicity Metrics}

To quantify predictive multiplicity at the level of individual observations, we use two
complementary metrics. Both capture disagreement among Rashomon models, but from
different angles: one reflects the spread of predicted probabilities, while the other reflects
how often models fall on opposite sides of a decision threshold. Predictive variance is the
primary metric because it preserves probability-space information even when all models
agree on the final label; the conflict ratio complements it by isolating decision-level
disagreement.

\paragraph{Predictive variance.}
The observation-wise predictive variance measures how much predicted probabilities vary
across the Rashomon set. Let $|\mathcal{R}_\varepsilon| = M$ and let $\hat{p}_{mi}$ denote
the predicted probability for observation $i$ from model
$m \in \mathcal{R}_\varepsilon$. The variance is
\[
v_i = \mathrm{Var}_{m \in \mathcal{R}_\varepsilon}(\hat{p}_{mi}),
\quad i = 1,\dots,N.
\]
High $v_i$ indicates that near-optimal models disagree substantially on observation $i$;
low $v_i$ indicates agreement. Unlike range-based or purely label-based disagreement
metrics, predictive variance reflects the full distribution of probabilities within the
Rashomon set.

\paragraph{Conflict ratio.}
\label{sec:meth-conflict}
As a complementary measure, the \emph{conflict ratio} captures hard-label disagreement.
Let $\tau = 0.5$ be a decision threshold and let
\[
q_i = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^{M} \mathbf{1}[\hat{p}_{mi} \geq \tau]
\]
denote the fraction of Rashomon models predicting a positive label for observation $i$.
The conflict ratio is
\[
c_i = \min(q_i,\; 1-q_i), \quad c_i \in [0, 0.5].
\]
A value $c_i = 0$ indicates unanimous agreement; $c_i = 0.5$ indicates a maximally
split ensemble. Because $c_i$ captures hard-label disagreement, it is directly related to
the variance of binary votes:
$\mathrm{Var}(\hat{y}_{mi}) = q_i(1-q_i) = c_i(1-c_i)$ when computed across models
\citep{frohnapfel_using_2026}. We apply the same spatial analysis (Moran's $I$, LISA)
to $c_i$ as to $v_i$, yielding conflict-based hotspot regions.

\subsection{Feature-Space Graph and Spatial Statistics}
\label{sec:meth-graph}

To study the spatial organization of predictive multiplicity, we represent the observations
of interest as a graph in feature space. Nodes correspond to observations, and edges encode
local neighborhood relationships. Throughout, ``spatial'' refers to local dependence in
this feature-space graph rather than to geographic space.

\paragraph{Graph construction.}
\label{sec:method-spatial}
A $k$-nearest-neighbor (kNN) graph is constructed using preprocessed feature vectors and
Euclidean distance. The graph is built on the same set of observations for which
multiplicity is evaluated, so that spatial clustering is assessed directly on the prediction
targets of interest. The initial graph is directed; we symmetrize the adjacency so that an
edge exists between $i$ and $j$ whenever either $i$ is among the $k$ nearest neighbors of
$j$ or vice versa. Let $A \in \{0,1\}^{N \times N}$ denote the symmetric binary adjacency
matrix. The spatial weight matrix $W$ is obtained by row-standardizing $A$:
\[
W_{ij} = \frac{A_{ij}}{\sum_{j'=1}^{N} A_{ij'}},
\quad i = 1,\dots,N.
\]
Each row sums to one, so the spatial lag of a variable $\mathbf{v}$ at observation $i$ is the
weighted average of its neighbors' values:
$[W\mathbf{v}]_i = \sum_j W_{ij} v_j$.
This lag enters both global Moran's $I$ and the local LISA statistics.

The neighborhood size $k$ is treated as a dataset-specific parameter and is chosen to
ensure graph connectivity and meaningful local structure. Its concrete value is specified in
Chapter~4.

\paragraph{Global Moran's $I$.}
\label{sec:meth-moran}
Global Moran's $I$ tests whether predictive variance is spatially structured: do
observations with similar variance tend to be neighbors in feature space? Let
$\mathbf{v} = (v_1,\dots,v_N)^\top$ and let $\bar{v}$ denote its mean. Moran's $I$ is
\[
I = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N W_{ij}(v_i-\bar{v})(v_j-\bar{v})}
{\sum_{i=1}^N (v_i-\bar{v})^2}.
\]
Positive $I$ indicates that high-variance observations tend to cluster together; values near
zero suggest spatial randomness. Significance is assessed by permuting the variance values
across observations while holding $W$ fixed, thereby yielding a null distribution for $I$.
This graph-level permutation should be distinguished from the prediction-matrix null in
Section~\ref{sec:meth-null}, which operates one level earlier by permuting model
predictions before the multiplicity field is constructed.

\paragraph{Local indicators (LISA) and HH hotspots.}
\label{sec:meth-lisa}
Global Moran's $I$ does not identify \emph{where} spatial dependence occurs. Local
Indicators of Spatial Association (LISA) localize it. For each observation $i$, let
$z_i = v_i - \bar{v}$ and let $l_i = [W\mathbf{z}]_i$ denote the spatial lag of the centered
variable. The local Moran statistic is
\[
I_i = z_i \cdot l_i.
\]
A conditional permutation test, in which the values $\{z_j : j \neq i\}$ are permuted
while $z_i$ and $W$ are held fixed, yields a pseudo $p$-value. The
Benjamini--Hochberg procedure at level $\alpha$ controls the false discovery rate across
all $N$ local tests.

Each significant observation is classified by the signs of $z_i$ and $l_i$:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{High--High (HH):} $z_i > 0$, $l_i > 0$ --- above-average variance and
  neighbors with above-average variance. These are the \emph{multiplicity hotspots}.
  \item \textbf{Low--Low (LL):} $z_i < 0$, $l_i < 0$ --- stable coldspots.
  \item \textbf{High--Low (HL):} high-variance outlier in a low-variance neighborhood.
  \item \textbf{Low--High (LH):} low-variance observation in a high-variance neighborhood.
\end{itemize}
Observations not reaching significance after correction are labeled \emph{non-significant}.
Throughout this thesis, ``HH region'' refers to the set of High--High observations.

\paragraph{Connected components of HH regions.}
To extract coherent regions, we restrict the kNN graph to HH observations and identify
connected components. Each component is a contiguous region of feature space with
elevated predictive variance. Very small components may be discarded to avoid treating
isolated fragments as substantive hotspot regions; the corresponding minimum-size
criterion is specified in Chapter~4. The resulting components support stability assessments
across splits and Rashomon set realizations.

\subsection{Null Model for Significance Testing}
\label{sec:meth-null}

To ensure that detected spatial structure is not merely an artifact of the marginal
distribution of disagreement values, we use a null model based on permutation of the
prediction matrix itself. The null hypothesis is that the overall magnitude of model
disagreement is preserved, but its alignment with feature-space location is random.

The null operates on the prediction matrix $P \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times N}$. For each model
$m$ independently, the vector $(\hat{p}_{m1}, \dots, \hat{p}_{mN})$ is randomly permuted
across observations. This \emph{within-model permutation} preserves each model's
marginal distribution of predictions while destroying systematic alignment between
predictions and feature-space location. Any spatial clustering of the resulting $v_i$ or
$c_i$ is therefore attributable to chance.

For each of $R$ permutations, observation-wise variance (and conflict ratio) and global
Moran's $I$ are recomputed. The empirical $p$-value is
\[
p = \frac{1 + \#\{\text{null} \geq \text{observed}\}}{R + 1}.
\]
This tests whether the observed spatial organization of disagreement exceeds what would
arise from spatially unstructured predictions of the same overall magnitude.


\subsection{Methodological Assumptions}
\label{sec:meth-assumptions}

The proposed framework relies on several assumptions.

First, the feature-space representation induces a meaningful notion of local similarity,
such that nearby observations are comparable in terms of predictive behavior. The spatial
analysis therefore depends on the choice of feature transformation and distance metric.

Second, the finite candidate pool and the top-$K$ selection provide a practical
approximation of the Rashomon set of near-optimal models. The resulting multiplicity
estimates therefore depend on the model families and hyperparameter configurations
included in the candidate pool.

Third, the analysis is descriptive rather than causal. Identified hotspot regions indicate
where predictive multiplicity concentrates, but do not explain the underlying
data-generating mechanisms that give rise to disagreement between models.

Finally, the framework is defined for probabilistic classification models and relies on
their predicted probabilities. Extensions to other prediction settings (e.g., regression or
multiclass classification) require corresponding adaptations of the multiplicity metrics.

\subsection{Summary}

The methodology provides a unified framework for analyzing predictive multiplicity as a
spatial phenomenon. Its core steps are: (1) Rashomon set construction, (2)
observation-wise predictive variance and conflict ratio, (3) feature-space kNN graph,
(4) global Moran's $I$ and local LISA for hotspot detection, (5) connected components
of HH regions, and (6) a permutation null for significance testing. Chapter~4 then
instantiates this framework on concrete datasets and experimental settings.
