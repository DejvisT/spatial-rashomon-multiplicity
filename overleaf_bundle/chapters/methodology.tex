
This chapter presents the methodology for analyzing predictive multiplicity through a spatial lens. The approach is independent of any particular dataset or experimental configuration; concrete instantiation is described in Chapter~4.

The methodological flow is as follows. We first construct a Rashomon set of near-optimal models and compute observation-wise multiplicity metrics (predictive variance and conflict ratio). We then build a feature-space graph to encode neighborhood relationships, quantify global and local spatial clustering via Moran's $I$ and LISA, extract hotspot regions (HH clusters) and their connected components, and finally compare observed structure against a permutation null to assess significance. This pipeline identifies \emph{where} in feature space predictive instability concentrates and whether that concentration exceeds chance.

\subsection{Problem Setting and Notation}

Let $(X, y)$ denote a supervised learning dataset with $N$ observations, where $X \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$ is the feature matrix and $y \in \{0,1\}^N$ is a binary target variable. Let $\mathcal{F}$ denote a hypothesis class of predictive models, potentially spanning multiple model families and hyperparameter configurations.

We consider probabilistic classifiers $f \in \mathcal{F}$ that output predicted probabilities $\hat{p}_{fi} \in [0,1]$ for each observation $i \in \{1,\dots,N\}$. Model performance is measured using a loss function $L(f)$ evaluated on held-out data.

\subsection{Rashomon Set Construction}
\label{sec:meth-rashomon}

Predictive multiplicity is studied through the Rashomon set, defined as the collection of models whose performance lies within a tolerance $\varepsilon > 0$ of the best achievable loss. In our setting, $\mathcal{F}$ is a \emph{finite} candidate pool: a set of models trained with varying families and hyperparameter configurations on a common training set. Let
\[
L^* = \min_{f \in \mathcal{F}} L(f)
\]
denote the minimum validation loss (Brier score) among all candidates. The Rashomon set is then
\[
\mathcal{R}_\varepsilon = \{ f \in \mathcal{F} : L(f) \leq L^* + \varepsilon \}.
\]

The tolerance $\varepsilon$ controls the strictness of selection. Because the exact value of $\varepsilon$ that produces a given set size varies across datasets and splits, we adopt an equivalent \textbf{top-$K$ approximation}: the $K$ models with the lowest validation Brier score are selected, yielding $|\mathcal{R}| = K$. This fixes the Rashomon set size across runs and datasets. Selection can be \emph{global} (top-$K$ across all families) or \emph{per-family} (top-$K$ within each model family); the latter is used when attributing multiplicity to hyperparameters (Chapter~4).

\subsection{Observation-Wise Multiplicity Metrics}

To quantify predictive multiplicity at the level of individual observations, we use two complementary metrics. Both capture disagreement among Rashomon models but from different angles: one reflects the spread of predicted probabilities, the other the fraction of models on each side of the decision threshold.

\paragraph{Predictive variance.}
The observation-wise predictive variance measures how much predicted probabilities vary across the Rashomon set. Let $|\mathcal{R}_\varepsilon| = M$ and let $\hat{p}_{mi}$ denote the predicted probability for observation $i$ from model $m \in \mathcal{R}_\varepsilon$. The variance is
\[
v_i = \mathrm{Var}_{m \in \mathcal{R}_\varepsilon}(\hat{p}_{mi}), \quad i = 1,\dots,N.
\]
High $v_i$ indicates that near-optimal models disagree substantially on observation $i$; low $v_i$ indicates agreement. Unlike range-based or binary disagreement metrics, predictive variance reflects the full distribution of predictions within the Rashomon set.

\paragraph{Conflict ratio.}
\label{sec:meth-conflict}
As a complementary measure, the \emph{conflict ratio} captures hard-label disagreement. Let $\tau = 0.5$ be a decision threshold and let
\[
  q_i = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^{M} \mathbf{1}[\hat{p}_{mi} \geq \tau]
\]
denote the fraction of Rashomon models predicting a positive label for observation~$i$. The conflict ratio is
\[
  c_i = \min(q_i,\; 1 - q_i), \quad c_i \in [0, 0.5].
\]
A value $c_i = 0$ indicates unanimous agreement; $c_i = 0.5$ indicates a maximally split ensemble. Because $c_i$ captures hard-label disagreement, it is directly related to the variance of binary votes: $\mathrm{Var}(\hat{y}_{mi}) = q_i(1-q_i) = c_i(1-c_i)$ when computed across models \citep{frohnapfel_using_2026}. We apply the same spatial analysis (Moran's $I$, LISA) to $c_i$ as to $v_i$, yielding conflict-based hotspot regions.

\subsection{Feature-Space Graph and Spatial Statistics}
\label{sec:meth-graph}

To study the spatial organization of predictive variance, we represent the dataset as a graph in feature space. Observations are nodes; edges encode local neighborhood relationships.

\paragraph{Graph construction.}
A $k$-nearest-neighbor (kNN) graph is constructed using (preprocessed) feature vectors with Euclidean distance. The initial graph is directed; we symmetrize the adjacency so that an edge exists between $i$ and $j$ whenever either $i$ is among the $k$ nearest neighbors of $j$ or vice versa. Let $A \in \{0,1\}^{N \times N}$ denote the symmetric binary adjacency matrix. The spatial weight matrix $W$ is obtained by row-standardizing $A$:
\[
  W_{ij} = \frac{A_{ij}}{\sum_{j'=1}^{N} A_{ij'}}, \quad i = 1, \dots, N.
\]
Each row sums to one, so the spatial lag of a variable $\mathbf{v}$ at observation $i$ is the weighted average of its neighbors' values: $[W\mathbf{v}]_i = \sum_j W_{ij} v_j$. This lag enters both global Moran's $I$ and the local LISA statistics.

The neighborhood size $k$ is treated as a dataset-specific parameter and is chosen to ensure graph connectivity and meaningful local structure.

\paragraph{Global Moran's $I$.}
\label{sec:meth-moran}
Global Moran's $I$ tests whether predictive variance is spatially structured: do observations with similar variance tend to be neighbors? Let $\mathbf{v} = (v_1, \dots, v_N)^\top$ and $\bar{v}$ its mean. Moran's $I$ is
\[
I = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N W_{ij} (v_i - \bar{v})(v_j - \bar{v})}
{\sum_{i=1}^N (v_i - \bar{v})^2}.
\]
Positive $I$ indicates that high-variance observations tend to cluster together; values near zero suggest spatial randomness. Significance is assessed via permutation of the variance values across observations (holding $W$ fixed), yielding a null distribution for $I$. (This is distinct from the observation-level permutation null in Section~\ref{sec:meth-null}, which permutes model predictions.)

\paragraph{Local indicators (LISA) and HH hotspots.}
\label{sec:meth-lisa}
Global Moran's $I$ does not identify \emph{where} spatial dependence occurs. Local Indicators of Spatial Association (LISA) localize it. For each observation $i$, let $z_i = v_i - \bar{v}$ and $l_i = [W\mathbf{z}]_i$ (the spatial lag of $z$). The local Moran statistic is
\[
  I_i = z_i \cdot l_i.
\]
A conditional permutation test (values $\{z_j : j \neq i\}$ permuted while $z_i$ and $W$ are fixed) yields a pseudo $p$-value. The Benjamini--Hochberg procedure at level $\alpha$ controls the false discovery rate across all $N$ tests.

Each significant observation is classified by the signs of $z_i$ and $l_i$:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{High--High (HH):} $z_i > 0$, $l_i > 0$ --- above-average variance \emph{and} neighbors with above-average variance. These are the \emph{multiplicity hotspots}.
  \item \textbf{Low--Low (LL):} $z_i < 0$, $l_i < 0$ --- stable coldspots.
  \item \textbf{High--Low (HL):} high-variance outlier in a low-variance neighborhood.
  \item \textbf{Low--High (LH):} low-variance observation in a high-variance neighborhood.
\end{itemize}
Observations not reaching significance after correction are labeled \emph{non-significant}. Throughout this thesis, ``HH region'' refers to the set of High--High observations.

\paragraph{Connected components of HH regions.}
To extract coherent regions, we restrict the kNN graph to HH observations and identify connected components. Each component is a contiguous region of feature space with elevated predictive variance. Components below a minimum size may be discarded. The resulting regions support stability assessments across splits and Rashomon set realizations.

\subsection{Null Model for Significance Testing}
\label{sec:meth-null}

To ensure that detected spatial structure is not an artifact of marginal variance distributions, we use a null model based on permutation of predictions.

The null operates on the prediction matrix $P \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times N}$. For each model $m$ independently, the vector $(\hat{p}_{m1}, \dots, \hat{p}_{mN})$ is randomly permuted across observations. This \emph{within-model permutation} preserves each model's marginal distribution of predictions but destroys alignment between predictions and feature-space location. Any spatial clustering of the resulting $v_i$ or $c_i$ is due to chance.

For each of $R$ permutations, observation-wise variance (and conflict ratio) and global Moran's $I$ are recomputed. The empirical $p$-value is
\[
  p = \frac{1 + \#\{\text{null} \geq \text{observed}\}}{R + 1}.
\]
This tests whether spatially structured disagreement exceeds what would arise from spatially unstructured predictions of the same magnitude.

\subsection{Summary}

The methodology provides a unified framework for analyzing predictive multiplicity as a spatial phenomenon. The core pipeline comprises: (1) Rashomon set construction, (2) observation-wise predictive variance and conflict ratio, (3) feature-space kNN graph, (4) global Moran's $I$ and local LISA for hotspot detection, (5) connected components of HH regions, and (6) permutation null for significance. Chapter~4 describes how this framework is instantiated on concrete datasets, including robustness checks (calibration, alternative graphs), validation on synthetic data, interpretation analyses (hyperparameter decomposition, rule extraction), and auxiliary diagnostics (decision-boundary proximity, fairness exposure).
