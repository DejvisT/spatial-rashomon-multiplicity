Predictive multiplicity refers to the phenomenon whereby multiple models achieve nearly identical performance yet produce meaningfully different predictions for the same individual.
Recent work shows this behavior is common in realistic pipelines and has direct implications for reliability, fairness, and interpretability \citep{black_model_2022}.
This chapter reviews the literature most relevant to this thesis and identifies the methodological gaps that our spatial-variance framework addresses. Each section concludes with a brief positioning statement on what remains missing and how this thesis fills the gap.

% -------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Rashomon Sets and Predictive Multiplicity}
% -------------------------------------------------------------------

The modern formulation of multiplicity is rooted in the \emph{Rashomon effect} named by \cite{breiman_statistical_2001}: many models can explain the same data equally well.
Let $\mathcal{F}$ be a hypothesis class, $L(f)$ a loss function, and $L^* = \min_{f \in \mathcal{F}} L(f)$.
The \emph{Rashomon set} is
\[
  \mathcal{R}_\varepsilon
  = \bigl\{f \in \mathcal{F} : L(f) \leq L^{*} + \varepsilon\bigr\}.
\]
This definition shifts attention from a single best model to a \emph{set} of near-optimal models.
In high-dimensional tabular settings this set can be large and structurally diverse \citep{semenova_existence_2022}.

\cite{rudin_amazing_2024} argue that when many near-optimal models exist, model selection should explicitly weigh secondary criteria---interpretability, fairness, robustness---rather than treating one selected model as uniquely correct.
\cite{semenova_existence_2022} formalize the \emph{Rashomon ratio} (volume of $\mathcal{R}_\varepsilon$ relative to the hypothesis space) and show that large ratios imply the existence of simpler, interpretable models at little accuracy cost.

\cite{marx_predictive_2020} coined the term \emph{predictive multiplicity} and introduced the first formal measures---\emph{ambiguity} and \emph{discrepancy}---for linear classifiers.
\cite{black_model_2022} subsequently broadened the scope, framing model multiplicity as a systemic challenge for accountability: if equally valid models disagree on individual decisions, which model's output should govern?

\paragraph{Positioning.}
Prior work establishes the Rashomon set as the central object for studying multiplicity, but does not address whether disagreement is spatially concentrated in feature space or how to localize and explain it.

\subsection{Metrics for Predictive Multiplicity}
\label{sec:metrics-review}
% -------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Label-space metrics.}
The ambiguity and discrepancy of \cite{marx_predictive_2020} operate on binary labels relative to a baseline model.
While intuitive, these metrics collapse probability information to a 0/1 threshold, potentially masking calibration-relevant variation.

\paragraph{Probability-space metrics.}
\cite{watson-daniels_predictive_2023} extend predictive multiplicity to \emph{probabilistic classification}, defining measures that capture variation in risk scores (predicted probabilities) rather than hard labels.
Their framework quantifies both the prevalence of conflicting risk scores and their severity across near-optimal convex models, and relates multiplicity to dataset characteristics such as separability and outlier structure.
Relatedly, \cite{hsu_rashomon_nodate} propose \emph{Rashomon Capacity}, a global, information-theoretic measure of how broadly the Rashomon set spans prediction space.
While Rashomon Capacity captures diversity at the dataset level, it does not resolve \emph{where} in feature space disagreement concentrates.

\paragraph{Per-instance metrics and the conflict ratio.}
Per-instance feasible ranges provide finer resolution.
\cite{hsu_rashomongb_2024} introduce the \emph{Viable Prediction Range} (VPR) for gradient boosting, estimating for each observation the min/max probability attainable inside $\mathcal{R}_\varepsilon$.
The \emph{conflict ratio} $c(x) = \min(q(x), 1-q(x))$, where $q(x)$ is the fraction of models predicting above a decision threshold, measures per-point label disagreement on a $[0, 0.5]$ scale.
\cite{frohnapfel_using_2026} argue that individual conflict ratios should be reported to comply with the EU AI Act's provisions on per-person accuracy, making the metric directly policy-relevant.

\paragraph{Scope.}
This thesis focuses on predictive multiplicity across near-optimal models in the Rashomon set, not on generic procedural randomness (e.g., self-consistency across repeated training runs with different seeds). The latter is orthogonal: we hold the candidate pool fixed and study how predictions vary within it.

\paragraph{Positioning.}
All of the above metrics are either global summaries or pointwise scalars; none identify \emph{spatially contiguous regions} of instability in feature space. Existing work measures \emph{how much} models disagree, but not \emph{where} they disagree in a statistically testable, localized way.

\subsection{Explaining and Attributing Disagreement Across Models}
\label{sec:vi-review}
% -------------------------------------------------------------------

Classical variable importance is model-specific: a feature is ``important'' for one fitted model but may be unimportant for another equally accurate model.
\cite{fisher_all_2019} address this with \emph{Model Class Reliance} (MCR), computing the range of permutation-importance values across all models in $\mathcal{R}_\varepsilon$.
\cite{dong_variable_2020} extend this idea to \emph{Variable Importance Clouds}, visualizing the joint distribution of importance scores for every feature across the set of good models.
\cite{donnelly_rashomon_2024} propose the \emph{Rashomon Importance Distribution} (RID), a framework that quantifies feature importance across $\mathcal{R}_\varepsilon$ with consistency guarantees under data perturbation.

These lines of work attribute variation in \emph{explanations} across models to individual features.
Our thesis pursues a complementary direction: attributing variation in \emph{predictions} (i.e., multiplicity) to model families and, within each family, to hyperparameters---a variance-decomposition approach that quantifies structural sources of disagreement rather than feature-level explanations.

\cite{cavus_role_2025} provide the closest antecedent to our hyperparameter analysis.
Studying six model families on 21 benchmark datasets, they show that tuning key hyperparameters (e.g., regularization strength, tree depth, learning rate) significantly increases prediction discrepancy within each family.
Our work builds on this insight by decomposing per-observation variance into between-family and within-family (hyperparameter) components, and by identifying which hyperparameters contribute most to disagreement inside localized regions.

\paragraph{Positioning.}
Prior work does not clearly separate the roles of model family versus hyperparameter variation, nor does it attribute disagreement specifically within localized high-multiplicity regions. This thesis fills that gap.

\subsection{Localizing Instability in Feature Space}
\label{sec:spatial-review}
% -------------------------------------------------------------------

Spatial statistics are well established for detecting non-random local structure in geospatial and networked data.
\emph{Global Moran's $I$} tests whether a continuous variable exhibits overall spatial autocorrelation under a neighborhood graph, while \emph{Local Indicators of Spatial Association} (LISA) decompose this into per-observation statistics that identify \emph{High--High} (HH) and \emph{Low--Low} (LL) clusters.
Our key methodological transfer is to replace the geographic adjacency matrix with a $k$-nearest-neighbor (kNN) graph in feature space, treating per-observation prediction variance as the spatial variable.

In the multiplicity literature, explicit feature-space localization remains underdeveloped.
Prior work overwhelmingly quantifies \emph{how much} models disagree, not \emph{where} they disagree in a contiguous, statistically testable way.
\cite{watson-daniels_predictive_2023} observe that multiplicity concentrates near decision boundaries and among outliers, but do not formalize localization via spatial statistics.
\cite{herbinger_repid_2022} detect regions with heterogeneous feature effects (REPID), which is conceptually related---both seek local structure---but targets single-model explanations rather than cross-model disagreement.

\paragraph{Positioning.}
This thesis operationalizes the missing localization step: we apply Moran's $I$ and LISA to per-point variance and conflict across the Rashomon set, yielding statistically significant hotspots that can be mapped, compared across seeds, and characterized by interpretable rules.

\subsection{Interpreting and Auditing Localized Multiplicity}
\label{sec:interp-review}
% -------------------------------------------------------------------

Interpretability research typically explains the decision boundary of a \emph{single} model.
Under multiplicity the central object is not one boundary but a \emph{region of instability} across many near-optimal models.
\cite{kobylinska_exploration_2023} propose \textsc{Rashomon\_DETECT} to identify models in the Rashomon set that behave differently, using a \emph{Profile Disparity Index} based on partial-dependence profiles.
Their goal is to flag heterogeneous explanations; our goal is to flag heterogeneous \emph{predictions at the observation level} and characterize the affected subpopulations.

Regional effect methods such as REPID \citep{herbinger_repid_2022} and forward marginal effects \citep{scholbeck_marginal_2024} partition feature space into regions with homogeneous feature effects.
We draw on a related principle---subgroup discovery via decision rules---but apply it to a different target: the localized high-multiplicity regions produced by our spatial analysis, rather than a single model's residuals.
The resulting rules describe \emph{who} is most subject to model arbitrariness, not why one model predicts a particular label.

Fairness analyses of multiplicity are a growing area. \cite{sokol_cross-model_2024} define \emph{cross-model fairness}: an individual harmed when one predictor is chosen ad hoc from a pool of equally performing models could argue for the model that grants the most favorable outcome. Our thesis does not propose new fairness constraints; instead, it provides a descriptive diagnostic---subgroup exposure analysis---that quantifies whether localized high-multiplicity regions disproportionately affect specific demographic groups.

Robustness of spatial findings to analyst choices (Rashomon-set size $K$, kNN neighborhood $k$, probability calibration) is essential. If hotspots disappear after calibration, they may reflect miscalibration rather than genuine disagreement. Our calibration-robustness analysis (Section~\ref{sec:results-calibration}) tests this directly.

\paragraph{Positioning.}
Prior work does not combine spatial localization with interpretable characterization of the affected subpopulations. This thesis connects multiplicity diagnosis to actionable subpopulation descriptions and robustness verification.

\subsection{Positioning of This Thesis}
% -------------------------------------------------------------------

Relative to prior work, this thesis contributes by integrating five strands that are usually studied in isolation:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Rashomon-set predictive multiplicity} \citep{marx_predictive_2020, black_model_2022}, operationalized with both variance and conflict metrics at the per-observation level;
    \item \textbf{Spatial localization} via Moran's $I$ and LISA applied to feature-space kNN graphs, transferring tools from spatial statistics to detect statistically significant hotspots of disagreement;
    \item \textbf{Source attribution} via hierarchical variance decomposition---first between model families, then within each family by hyperparameter---building on the insights of \cite{cavus_role_2025};
    \item \textbf{Interpretable characterization} of instability regions through decision-rule extraction, connecting multiplicity diagnosis to actionable subpopulation descriptions;
    \item \textbf{Robustness diagnostics} covering sensitivity to $K$, $k$, calibration, and cross-seed stability of hotspot locations.
\end{enumerate}

The resulting perspective treats predictive multiplicity as a \emph{localized, structured phenomenon} rather than a single global nuisance metric. Existing work mostly measures multiplicity globally; this thesis studies whether multiplicity forms localized regions in feature space and how those regions can be explained, attributed to modeling choices, and characterized for practitioners.
