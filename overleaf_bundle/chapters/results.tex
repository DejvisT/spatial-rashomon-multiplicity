This chapter presents the main empirical results. We begin with a cross-dataset overview of predictive multiplicity and its spatial structure (Section~\ref{sec:results-metrics}), then compare soft prediction variance with hard conflict ratio (Section~\ref{sec:results-conflict}), test the significance of the observed clustering against a permutation null (Section~\ref{sec:results-null}), and examine the stability of hotspot regions across runs (Section~\ref{sec:results-spatial}). We next compare global and per-family Rashomon sets (Section~\ref{sec:results-global-vs-family}), analyze robustness to analytical choices including $K$, $k$, calibration, and graph construction (Section~\ref{sec:results-robustness}), and then study model-family and hyperparameter drivers of multiplicity (Section~\ref{sec:results-hp}). The chapter concludes with synthetic validation, interpretable rule extraction, and auxiliary diagnostic analyses. Unless stated otherwise, all analyses use the global Rashomon set (top-$K$ with $K=25$) and the default spatial parameters defined in Chapter~4.

\subsection{Multiplicity and Spatial Metrics by Dataset}
\label{sec:results-metrics}

We begin with a compact cross-dataset summary of the two main questions underlying this thesis: how large predictive multiplicity is, and how strongly it is spatially structured in feature space. Table~\ref{tab:dataset_summary} reports the core summary statistics over 10 outer runs for COMPAS, German Credit, and Adult.

The three datasets differ clearly in both the magnitude and the spatial organization of predictive multiplicity. COMPAS exhibits the strongest spatial clustering, with the largest mean Moran's $I$ ($0.20 \pm 0.08$), indicating that high-variance observations are concentrated in localized regions of the feature space. German Credit shows weaker spatial clustering ($0.10 \pm 0.04$), but the largest overall disagreement magnitude, with the highest mean predictive variance and conflict-related values. Adult, in contrast, displays comparatively low disagreement magnitude and the weakest Moran's $I$ ($0.07 \pm 0.02$), though the clustering signal remains consistently positive across runs.

The HH hotspot counts provide additional context, but should be interpreted with care. In absolute terms, Adult contains the largest number of HH observations ($286.1$ on average), followed by COMPAS ($86.7$), while German Credit contains very few HH points. However, these counts are not directly comparable across datasets because both dataset size and the dataset-specific neighborhood parameter $k$ differ. For this reason, Moran's $I$ is the more informative cross-dataset measure of spatial concentration. Under this interpretation, COMPAS shows the clearest localized hotspot structure, whereas Adult is better characterized by broader and more diffuse regions of instability.

\subsection{Conflict Ratio vs Soft Prediction Variance}
\label{sec:results-conflict}

In addition to soft prediction variance $v_i$, which captures the spread of predicted probabilities across Rashomon models, we also consider the conflict ratio $c_i$, which measures hard-label disagreement relative to the 0.5 decision threshold. The two metrics capture complementary aspects of predictive multiplicity: variance reflects disagreement in probability space, whereas conflict isolates disagreement that changes the predicted class.

\subsubsection{Why Both Metrics Are Needed}

The distinction is substantively important. An observation can have high variance without high conflict if predicted probabilities vary widely but remain on the same side of the decision threshold. Conversely, an observation can have high conflict despite relatively low variance if predictions are tightly concentrated near 0.5 and small shifts are sufficient to flip the label. Reporting both metrics therefore separates probability-level instability from decision-level disagreement.

\subsubsection{Dataset-Level Conflict Metrics}

Table~\ref{tab:conflict_summary} summarizes conflict-related metrics across datasets. The overall pattern mirrors the variance-based results, but also highlights important differences between disagreement magnitude and spatial concentration. German Credit exhibits the largest overall conflict, with the highest mean $c_i$ and the largest fraction of conflicted observations. However, its conflict Moran's $I$ remains relatively small, indicating that hard-label disagreement is widespread but only weakly clustered in feature space. COMPAS shows lower overall conflict prevalence than German Credit, but substantially stronger spatial concentration ($0.17 \pm 0.03$), again indicating more localized disagreement regions. Adult shows the lowest overall conflict magnitude, but its conflict-based spatial clustering remains clearly positive and stronger than on German Credit, although still weaker than on COMPAS. Its large absolute number of conflict HH observations should be interpreted in light of the larger Adult test set and the larger neighborhood parameter k = 60.

Thus, high disagreement frequency does not necessarily imply strong spatial structure. German Credit is the clearest example: conflict is common, but not strongly localized. COMPAS combines substantial conflict with clear spatial concentration, while Adult occupies an intermediate position.

\subsubsection{Overlap Between Variance-Based and Conflict-Based Hotspots}

The overlap between variance-based and conflict-based HH hotspots is limited across all datasets. On COMPAS, the Jaccard similarity is only $0.06 \pm 0.05$, showing that the two metrics identify largely different observations as hotspots. This is consistent with their definitions: variance-based hotspots reflect broad probability spread, whereas conflict-based hotspots reflect threshold-crossing disagreement. Adult shows similarly low overlap ($0.10 \pm 0.05$), and although the mean overlap is numerically higher on German Credit ($0.21 \pm 0.42$), this estimate is unstable because both hotspot sets are often very small. Overall, the two metrics should therefore be interpreted as complementary rather than interchangeable.

\subsubsection{Quadrant Analysis}
\label{sec:results-quadrant}

To characterize the joint behavior of variance and conflict more explicitly, we partition observations into four quadrants using the 90th percentile of each metric within each run. This yields four regimes: jointly high variance and conflict, high variance only, high conflict only, and jointly low disagreement.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{A (high variance, high conflict):} both soft and hard disagreement are elevated.
  \item \textbf{B (high variance, low conflict):} models differ in probability space but agree on the label.
  \item \textbf{C (low variance, high conflict):} predictions are tightly clustered near 0.5, so small differences lead to label flips.
  \item \textbf{D (low variance, low conflict):} stable observations with minimal disagreement.
\end{itemize}

Table~\ref{tab:quadrant_compas} reports the resulting breakdown for COMPAS. Most observations fall into the stable regime D ($81.2\%$), showing that large disagreement remains concentrated in a minority of cases. Among the remaining observations, quadrants B ($7.7\%$) and C ($8.7\%$) are both more common than quadrant A ($2.3\%$). This indicates that high disagreement often appears in only one of the two metrics: either as broad probability variation without label changes, or as threshold-sensitive label instability without especially large probability spread.

\subsection{Null Hypothesis Testing}
\label{sec:results-null}

To test whether the observed spatial structure could arise by chance, we compare the observed Moran's $I$ values to a permutation-based null distribution (Section~\ref{sec:exp-null}). In this null model, each model's test-set predictions are independently permuted across observations ($R=100$ permutations per run), preserving the marginal prediction distribution of each model while removing any systematic alignment between disagreement and feature-space location.

Table~\ref{tab:null_significance} summarizes the null-test results. The conclusion is unambiguous: all three datasets exhibit significant variance-based Moran's $I$ in all 10 runs. In every case, the empirical permutation p-value reaches the minimum possible value under $R=100$ permutations, namely $p = 1/101 \approx 0.0099$.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Fraction of runs with significant Moran's $I$ ($p < 0.05$) under the permutation null, by dataset.}
\label{tab:null_significance}
\input{presentation_assets/tab/null_significance.tex}
\end{table}

These results show that the spatial clustering of predictive multiplicity is not a byproduct of the marginal distribution of disagreement values alone. Instead, disagreement is systematically aligned with feature-space structure. The strength of this clustering differs across datasets, but its statistical significance is uniformly strong.

Figure~\ref{fig:null_moran_example} illustrates this result for one representative COMPAS run. The observed Moran's $I$ lies well outside the null distribution, showing that the measured clustering cannot be explained by random reassignment of predictions to observations.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{null_moran_compas_seed0.pdf}
\caption{Null distribution of Moran's $I$ (100 permutations) versus the observed value for COMPAS seed 0. The observed value (vertical line) exceeds all null samples ($p < 0.01$).}
\label{fig:null_moran_example}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Conflict-Based Null Testing}

We repeat the same null experiment using the pointwise conflict ratio $c_i$ rather than predictive variance as the spatial variable. The results closely mirror the variance-based analysis. On COMPAS, conflict Moran's $I$ averages $0.17 \pm 0.03$, while German Credit and Adult again show weaker but consistently positive clustering. Under the permutation null, conflict Moran's I is significant in all runs for COMPAS and Adult, and in most runs for German Credit. Thus, the conflict-based clustering result is strongest for COMPAS and Adult, while German Credit again shows a weaker and less stable spatial signal.

This shows that the null result is not specific to soft probability disagreement. Spatial clustering also appears for hard-label disagreement, indicating that localized multiplicity is visible both in score space and in thresholded classification outcomes.

\subsubsection{Aggregated Null Summary}

Figure~\ref{fig:null_summary} summarizes the null experiment across all seeds for both variance-based and conflict-based Moran's $I$. The left panel compares observed Moran's $I$ to the corresponding null mean and standard deviation, while the right panel reports the associated z-scores.

Across all datasets, the observed values are clearly separated from the null distribution. COMPAS shows the largest separation, while Adult exhibits smaller but still clearly positive deviations. Thus, differences across datasets concern the \emph{strength} of clustering, not its existence: in every dataset, the observed spatial structure is far stronger than expected under a spatially unstructured null model.

\label{fig:null_summary}

Taken together, the permutation tests provide strong evidence that predictive multiplicity is spatially structured rather than randomly distributed across the feature space. This holds for both soft prediction variance and hard conflict, and across all three benchmark datasets.

\subsection{Spatial Structure and Stability of Hotspots}
\label{sec:results-spatial}

Having established that predictive variance is spatially clustered, we now examine how stable the resulting HH hotspots are across runs on COMPAS. Two levels of stability must be distinguished. First, \emph{point-level stability}: for a fixed test observation, how often is it classified as HH across random training seeds? Second, \emph{region-level stability}: do the same broader hotspot areas reappear across seeds even when the exact HH membership changes? All cross-seed comparisons in this section use the fixed-test-set protocol from Section~\ref{sec:exp-splits}, so that observation identities are aligned across runs.

\subsubsection{Point-level stability: HH selection frequency}

Let $f_i$ denote the fraction of outer seeds (out of 10) in which test point $i$ is classified as HH under the global Rashomon set (top-$K=25$) and the default neighborhood definition. Figure~\ref{fig:hh_location} shows HH membership in one representative run, while Figure~\ref{fig:hh_stability_freq} visualizes the corresponding per-point HH frequency in PCA space.

At the point level, stability is limited. Only about $2.0\%$ of the fixed test set satisfies $f_i \geq 0.8$, and only about $4.5\%$ satisfies $f_i \geq 0.5$. More broadly, the total \emph{HH support}---the set of observations classified as HH in at least one seed---covers about $17.2\%$ of the fixed test set. Within this support, HH membership is typically infrequent: the mean frequency is $0.33$, the median is $0.20$, and about $60.1\%$ of support points lie in the lowest frequency bucket $(0, 0.2]$. Defining a recurrent \emph{HH core} as points with $f_i \geq 0.5$, the core-to-support ratio is about $26.2\%$, indicating that only a minority of ever-detected HH points recur in at least half of the runs.

The main conclusion is therefore that \textbf{individual observations are not stably labeled as hotspots}. Exact HH membership varies substantially across seeds. At the same time, this variability is not spatially diffuse: high-$f_i$ values remain concentrated in one part of the PCA plane rather than being scattered uniformly. Thus, even though pointwise hotspot assignment is unstable, the broader location of multiplicity remains informative.

\subsubsection{Overlap of exact HH sets (pointwise Jaccard)}

To quantify agreement on the exact set of hotspot observations, we compare the HH masks between pairs of seeds using the Jaccard index
\[
J_{ss'} = \frac{|H_s \cap H_{s'}|}{|H_s \cup H_{s'}|}.
\]
Figure~\ref{fig:hh_jaccard} shows the resulting overlap matrix for variance-based HH masks on COMPAS. The mean off-diagonal Jaccard similarity is about 0.34, with values ranging from approximately 0.05 to 0.62 across seed pairs. Thus, exact HH membership is not perfectly stable, but neither is it random or negligible. Instead, the results indicate a recurring subset of hotspot observations together with substantial boundary variation across runs.

This complements the frequency-based result: exact HH membership is only partly recurrent and remains sensitive to seed-specific variation. However, this should not be interpreted as evidence against stable spatial structure. Pointwise Jaccard overlap is highly sensitive to boundary variation, so even modest shifts at the edges of a hotspot can greatly reduce overlap in the full HH mask. Low overlap of exact HH labels is therefore compatible with recurrence of the same broader hotspot regions, which is the question addressed next.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{hh_jaccard_heatmap_compas.pdf}
\caption{Pairwise Jaccard overlap of variance-based HH masks between seeds (COMPAS, fixed test). Diagonal entries equal one by construction; off-diagonal values show moderate but variable agreement in exact HH membership across runs.}
\label{fig:hh_jaccard}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Regional structure: connected components and cross-seed recurrence}

To move beyond individual HH points, we extract connected components of HH nodes in the same $k$NN graph used for LISA, discarding components smaller than 5 points. Table~\ref{tab:hh_component_summary} summarizes these component statistics across runs. On COMPAS, HH points typically form about $3$--$4$ connected components per run, with one dominant component whose maximum size averages about $44$ test points (median $34$). Hotspots therefore appear as a small number of contiguous regions rather than as isolated singletons.

We then compare these regions across seeds. For each pair of runs, each component in one run is matched to the most similar component in the other run using Jaccard overlap on the corresponding fixed test observations. The mean best-match component Jaccard is about 0.35, indicating moderate recurrence of component-level hotspot regions. This value is similar to the pointwise HH-mask overlap, so the component analysis should not be interpreted as proving much stronger stability than the raw HH masks. Rather, its main contribution is to show that detected HH points tend to form contiguous graph regions instead of isolated observations. Thus, exact HH membership and component-level regions both show partial recurrence, but also substantial seed-to-seed variability.

A component-matching view gives a similar qualitative picture. Some hotspot components have recognizable counterparts in other runs, but the degree of recurrence depends on the chosen matching threshold and reference run. For this reason, the component analysis is used descriptively rather than as a formal stability guarantee.

Taken together, the connected-component results show that HH observations are not isolated singletons, but often form contiguous regions in the feature-space graph. However, the exact boundaries and memberships of these regions vary across seeds. The stability evidence should therefore be interpreted cautiously: it supports a regional view of multiplicity, but does not imply that hotspot membership is fixed at the level of individual observations.

GBM and random forest occupy an intermediate position. Taken together, these comparisons support two conclusions. First, the families that drive structured disagreement are dataset-dependent rather than fixed across problems. Second, global pooling can mask substantial family-specific multiplicity patterns, particularly on COMPAS and Adult. This motivates the more explicit variance-decomposition analysis in the following section, where disagreement is attributed directly to model family and within-family hyperparameters.

\subsection{Robustness to Analytical Choices}
\label{sec:results-robustness}

We next assess whether the main spatial conclusions depend on several key analytical choices: the Rashomon set size $K$, the neighborhood size $k$ used in the feature-space graph, post-hoc probability calibration, and the precise graph construction itself. Across all four checks, the absolute magnitude of disagreement and the extent of detected hotspot regions can change, but the central conclusion remains stable: predictive multiplicity retains non-random spatial structure under a wide range of reasonable specifications.

\subsubsection{Sensitivity to Rashomon Set Size $K$}
\label{sec:results-sensitivity-K}

We first vary the Rashomon set size over $K \in \{5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 35, 40, 45, 50\}$ using global top-$K$ selection. Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity_K} shows the resulting sensitivity curves for COMPAS; the corresponding plots for German Credit and Adult follow the same qualitative pattern.

As expected, the overall magnitude of disagreement increases with $K$. Mean predictive variance rises monotonically as additional near-optimal models are included, and mean conflict shows the same behavior. In contrast, the spatial organization of disagreement is much more stable. On COMPAS, Moran's $I$ stabilizes once $K$ reaches roughly $20$--$25$, remaining in a narrow range thereafter, and the number of HH hotspots also changes only modestly. German Credit and Adult show the same qualitative behavior: disagreement magnitude grows as the Rashomon set widens, but the spatial pattern changes much less than the raw scale of disagreement.

The main implication is that larger Rashomon sets primarily add \emph{more} disagreement, not a fundamentally different \emph{structure} of disagreement. This supports the use of $K=25$ as a default: it is large enough to capture the relevant spatial organization of predictive multiplicity without substantially inflating the model set beyond the point where the qualitative conclusions have already stabilized.

\subsubsection{Sensitivity to kNN Neighborhood Size $k$}
\label{sec:results-sensitivity-kNN}

We next vary the neighborhood size $k$ used in the feature-space graph and recompute the variance-based and conflict-based spatial metrics. Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity_kNN} shows the resulting curves for COMPAS; the same qualitative behavior is observed on German Credit and Adult.

Mean predictive variance is invariant to $k$, since it is defined before the graph is constructed. By contrast, Moran's $I$ decreases as $k$ grows, reflecting the smoothing effect of larger neighborhoods. On COMPAS, variance-based Moran's $I$ falls from roughly $0.29$ at small $k$ to about $0.12$ at $k=100$, and conflict Moran's $I$ shows a similar decline. At the same time, HH counts tend to increase with larger neighborhoods on COMPAS and Adult, because broader neighborhoods can merge local structure into larger statistically significant regions. German Credit again shows the same direction of effect, though at a much smaller absolute scale.

Crucially, the clustering signal remains positive throughout the examined range. Thus, while the precise extent of detected hotspots depends on the neighborhood definition, the existence of spatial structure does not. The chosen defaults ($k=30$ for COMPAS and German Credit, $k=60$ for Adult) therefore represent a reasonable compromise between locality and graph stability rather than a finely tuned requirement for obtaining positive results.

\subsubsection{Calibration Robustness}
\label{sec:results-calibration}

To test whether the observed hotspot structure is primarily driven by probability miscalibration, we apply post-hoc calibration to each model in the global Rashomon set and then recompute the multiplicity and spatial metrics on the calibrated predictions. Figure~\ref{fig:calibration-delta-boxplots} summarizes the resulting run-level changes.

Across datasets, calibration changes the \emph{magnitude} of disagreement more than its \emph{spatial existence}. On German Credit, the effects are again mixed and more variable, which is unsurprising given the small size of the HH sets. On Adult, calibration can increase HH counts noticeably, but changes in Moran's $I$ remain modest in absolute terms. In all three datasets, the Jaccard overlap between pre- and post-calibration hotspot masks shows that exact HH membership can change, yet the broader hotspot regions remain recognizable.

Overall, calibration modifies the scale and boundaries of the detected hotspots, but it does not remove the underlying clustering of predictive multiplicity. This supports the interpretation that the main results reflect structural disagreement among near-optimal models rather than a mere artifact of uncalibrated probabilities.

\subsection{Hyperparameter and Family Importance}
\label{sec:results-hp}

To identify which modeling choices drive predictive multiplicity, we distinguish three levels
of explanation: differences between model families, differences between hyperparameter
settings within a family, and changes in these hyperparameter effects inside hotspot
regions. Family importance is assessed through a variance decomposition over model
family, whereas within-family hyperparameter importance is measured using the
model-level disagreement contribution $V_m$. A final diagnostic analysis examines
whether these effects are mainly explained by validation performance.

\subsubsection{Family Importance}

Model family explains a substantial share of predictive multiplicity across all datasets. Figure~\ref{fig:family_importance_agg} shows the between-family and within-family components of predictive variance within the Rashomon set, separately for all observations, HH hotspots, and non-HH observations. The between-family component is large in all three datasets, indicating that disagreement among near-optimal models is strongly structured by broad model-class choices rather than only by hyperparameter variation within a fixed family.

The strongest family effect appears on Adult, where the between-family share is about $0.71$ over all test observations and increases to about $0.79$ inside HH regions. COMPAS shows a lower but still substantial family contribution, increasing from about $0.41$ over all observations to about $0.52$ inside HH regions. German Credit has a similarly high all-observation family share of about $0.53$, but the HH and non-HH estimates are very similar; because German Credit contains only few HH observations, its hotspot-specific decomposition should be interpreted cautiously.

Comparing the Rashomon set with the full candidate pool on all observations shows that family structure is not simply a property of the entire search space. On Adult, family importance is much larger in the Rashomon set than in the full pool ($0.71$ vs. $0.36$), and German Credit shows the same pattern ($0.53$ vs. $0.30$). On COMPAS, by contrast, the Rashomon and full-pool values are very similar ($0.41$ vs. $0.42$). Thus, the role of model family depends on the dataset: in Adult and German Credit, near-optimal selection sharpens between-family differences, whereas on COMPAS family-level disagreement is already present in the broader candidate pool.

Taken together, these results support the interpretation that predictive multiplicity is partly driven by model-family choice. This effect is especially pronounced in Adult and remains substantial on COMPAS and German Credit. Moreover, for Adult and COMPAS, the family contribution increases inside HH regions, suggesting that hotspot predictions are particularly sensitive to the choice among model classes.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{family_importance_agg_rashomon.pdf}
\caption{Family importance within the Rashomon set. Bars show the mean share of total predictive variance attributable to between-family differences, with the remaining share corresponding to within-family variation. Error bars show standard deviations over seeds.}
\label{fig:family_importance_agg}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Within-Family Hyperparameter Drivers}

After separating between-family from within-family variation, we next ask which hyperparameters explain the remaining disagreement inside each model family. For each family-specific Rashomon set, we compute the model-level disagreement contribution $V_m$ and decompose variation in $V_m$ by hyperparameter groups. Categorical and low-cardinality numeric hyperparameters are grouped by exact value, while high-cardinality numeric hyperparameters are grouped into tertiles. Hyperparameters with insufficient group support are excluded for the corresponding seed.

Figure~\ref{fig:hp_decomp_rashomon} shows the resulting within-family hyperparameter importance values. The main pattern is that within-family disagreement is not diffuse across all hyperparameters. Instead, each family has a small number of dominant drivers. For logistic regression, regularization strength $C$ explains nearly all within-family variation across all three datasets, while the elastic-net mixing parameter $l_1$ ratio contributes much less. For kNN, the number of neighbors is the dominant driver in every dataset, indicating that the locality scale of the classifier is central to its multiplicity behavior.

Tree-based and neural-network families show more distributed but still interpretable patterns. For GBM, learning rate and tree-depth related parameters are consistently among the strongest drivers, although their ordering differs across datasets. Random forests are more dataset-dependent: maximum depth is strongest on COMPAS, while depth, feature subsampling, and leaf-size related parameters all contribute on German Credit and Adult. For MLPs, learning-rate initialization and hidden-layer size are the most prominent drivers, with regularization and activation contributing more moderately.

Overall, these results show that within-family multiplicity is structured by identifiable modeling choices rather than by random variation across the hyperparameter search space. Simpler families are often governed by one dominant complexity parameter, whereas more flexible families distribute disagreement across several architectural, regularization, or optimization-related parameters.

\label{fig:hp_decomp_rashomon}

\subsubsection{Hotspot-Specific Drivers}

The preceding analysis identifies which hyperparameters explain within-family disagreement on average. We next examine whether these effects change inside multiplicity hotspots. For each hyperparameter, we compare its $V_m$-based importance on HH observations with its importance on all test observations:
\[
\Delta \rho(h) = \rho_{\mathrm{HH}}(h) - \rho_{\mathrm{all}}(h).
\]
Positive values indicate that the hyperparameter explains a larger share of within-family disagreement inside HH regions, whereas negative values indicate lower importance inside hotspots.

Table~\ref{tab:hp_hotspot_delta_compas} reports the largest positive and negative shifts for COMPAS, which is the clearest real-data case of localized hotspot structure. The results suggest that hyperparameter effects are not spatially uniform. For GBM, subsampling becomes more important inside HH regions, while the number of estimators decreases slightly in importance. For MLP, learning-rate initialization shows the largest positive shift, suggesting that optimization-related choices contribute more strongly inside the most unstable regions. In contrast, random forest depth and kNN weighting become less important inside HH regions. Logistic regression shows no positive shift, indicating that its already dominant regularization effect does not become stronger inside COMPAS hotspots.

These shifts should be interpreted descriptively rather than causally. They indicate that the relative sources of within-family disagreement can differ between hotspot and non-hotspot regions, but several estimates have substantial cross-seed variability. HH-specific estimates for datasets with very small hotspot regions, especially German Credit, are therefore treated as auxiliary diagnostics rather than primary evidence.

\subsubsection{Conditional Analysis and Meta-Model Insights}

As a final diagnostic, we examine whether the hyperparameter effects identified above mainly reflect differences in validation performance. For each dataset--family combination, we fit a model-level meta-model predicting the disagreement contribution $V_m$ from grouped hyperparameters and validation Brier score. This analysis is not used as the primary attribution result, because validation Brier score is not a hyperparameter and the meta-model itself is descriptive rather than causal. Its purpose is instead to assess whether model disagreement is dominated by performance differences.

Table~\ref{tab:hp_meta_pool_summary} compares the Rashomon set with the full candidate pool. In the full pool, validation Brier score is the strongest predictor in 11 of 15 dataset--family combinations, with an average importance of $0.596$. This indicates that, when all trained candidates are included, validation performance differences explain a large share of model-level disagreement. Within the Rashomon set, this effect is weaker: validation Brier score is rank 1 in 7 of 15 combinations and its mean importance decreases to $0.290$. At the same time, the strongest non-performance hyperparameter has a higher mean importance in the Rashomon set ($0.498$) than in the full pool ($0.285$).

These results suggest that the Rashomon restriction reduces, but does not eliminate, the role of performance differences. Disagreement inside the near-optimal set is therefore not simply a byproduct of poorly performing models. However, validation Brier score remains important in several dataset--family combinations, especially for GBM, MLP, and parts of kNN. We therefore interpret the meta-model analysis as a performance-control diagnostic rather than as primary evidence for hyperparameter importance.

Overall, the driver analysis supports a hierarchical interpretation of predictive multiplicity. Model family explains a substantial share of disagreement, especially inside hotspot regions. Within model families, a smaller number of hyperparameters account for much of the remaining variation. The meta-model diagnostics indicate that these patterns are not reducible to validation-performance differences alone, although all such conditional importance results should be interpreted descriptively rather than causally.

\subsection{Synthetic Data Validation}
\label{sec:results-synthetic}

To validate the proposed pipeline, we evaluate it on synthetic datasets with known ground-truth ambiguous regions. These experiments test whether LISA-based hotspot detection can recover regions of inherent predictive uncertainty.

\subsubsection{Single-Island Design}

The single-island synthetic dataset consists of $n=3000$ observations in a two-dimensional feature space $(x_1, x_2)$. It contains two well-separated Gaussian clusters that form stable regions ($n=2400$ in total) and one ambiguous island ($n=600$), generated as a uniform disk with $P(y=1 \mid x) \approx 0.5$. We apply the standard experimental pipeline with five model families (LogReg, kNN, RF, GBM, MLP), 50 candidates per family (250 total), and a global top-$K=25$ Rashomon set. Evaluation is performed on a held-out test set of size $600$.

Figure~\ref{fig:synthetic_setup} shows the data-generating process and the ground-truth ambiguous region. Figure~\ref{fig:synthetic_variance_lisa} shows the resulting pointwise predictive variance together with the detected LISA HH hotspots. The variance map reveals that multiplicity is concentrated in the central island rather than in the two stable Gaussian regions, and the HH mask closely follows this structure.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{single_island_ground_truth.pdf}
    \caption{Synthetic single-island dataset. Two stable Gaussian clusters (left/right) and one ambiguous island (center, dashed circle) with $P(y=1 \mid x) \approx 0.5$.}
    \label{fig:synthetic_setup}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[htbp]

This spatial concentration is also reflected in the global autocorrelation statistic. The observed Moran’s $I$ is $0.2536$, indicating clear positive spatial clustering of predictive variance. Under the permutation null, the null mean is $-0.0024$ with standard deviation $0.0098$, and the one-sided empirical p-value is $0.0099$. Thus, the observed clustering is far larger than expected under spatial randomness, confirming that the multiplicity pattern is induced by the ambiguous island rather than by chance.

At the local level, LISA identifies $99$ HH points and $405$ LL points. Relative to the ground-truth island, which contains $120$ test observations, this yields a precision of $0.9697$, a recall of $0.8000$, and a Jaccard overlap of $0.7805$. In absolute terms, $96$ of the $99$ detected HH points lie inside the ambiguous island. This shows that the detected hotspot is highly specific to the intended ambiguous region, while still recovering most of its extent.

Figure~\ref{fig:synthetic_fdr} shows that the results are stable across this range. As $\alpha$ increases, the number of HH points rises moderately from $88$ to $109$, while the Jaccard overlap with the ground-truth island increases from $0.72$ to $0.79$. This change reflects the expected precision--recall trade-off: precision decreases only slightly, from about $0.99$ at $\alpha=0.01$ to about $0.93$ at $\alpha=0.20$, whereas recall increases from $0.73$ to $0.84$. Across all tested $\alpha$ values, the detected HH region remains a single connected component, matching the geometry of the true island.

    \label{fig:synthetic_fdr}
\end{figure}

Overall, the single-island experiment provides a controlled validation of the pipeline. It shows that when predictive ambiguity is concentrated in one coherent region, the combination of pointwise variance, Moran’s $I$, and LISA recovers that region accurately and with strong statistical support.

\subsection{Interpretable Rules for High-Multiplicity Regions}
\label{sec:results-rules}

To make multiplicity hotspots more actionable, we extract simple rules that describe HH regions (LISA High--High hotspots) in terms of the original input features. The goal is not to fully explain every hotspot point, but to assess whether localized regions of predictive multiplicity correspond to recognizable subpopulations.

\subsubsection{Rule Extraction Approach}

We use shallow decision trees (maximum depth 3) to extract human-readable rules. Trees are fitted on the original feature space, and positive leaf nodes are converted into conjunctive rules. Each rule is evaluated by support, purity, recall, and lift.

We apply this procedure in two ways. First, we fit global HH rules, where the target is membership in the full HH set of a given run. In this case, purity is defined as
\[
P(\text{HH} \mid \text{rule region}),
\]
and lift is the ratio between this purity and the HH base rate.

Second, we fit component-level rules, where each connected HH component is treated as the positive class and all other test observations as the negative class. In this case, purity is defined as
\[
P(\text{component} \mid \text{rule region}),
\]
and lift is measured relative to the base rate of the corresponding component. The component-level analysis is especially relevant because earlier results show that hotspots are better interpreted as localized regions than as pointwise-stable labels.

\subsubsection{Results on COMPAS}

COMPAS provides the clearest interpretable structure. The global HH rules reveal a strong support--purity trade-off: highly pure rules exist, but they typically describe small subgroups, whereas broader rules have lower purity. Figure~\ref{fig:rules_support_purity} shows this relationship. This indicates that the full HH set is heterogeneous and cannot be summarized well by a single global rule.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.58\textwidth]{rules_support_purity_compas.pdf}
\caption{Support--purity trade-off for global HH rules on COMPAS, aggregated across seeds. High-purity rules exist, but they usually cover only small subgroups.}
\label{fig:rules_support_purity}
\end{figure}

The component-level analysis gives a stronger regional interpretation. Across retained COMPAS hotspot components, shallow-tree rules often identify compact subregions with high enrichment. In the current analysis, 17 retained HH components across 8 seeds yield 36 component-level rules. Considering the highest-purity rule for each component, the median component size is 31 observations, the median purity is 0.53, the median recall is 0.96, and the median lift is 26.7. Thus, individual hotspot components are often more cleanly describable than the full global HH set.

Table~\ref{tab:component_rules_compas} reports representative component-level rules. The strongest rules mostly involve age and prior offence count. For example, rules based on high values of \texttt{priors\_count}, sometimes combined with higher age thresholds, identify compact regions with very high lift relative to the component base rate. A purity of 1.0 means that all observations satisfying the rule belong to the corresponding component, while recall below 1.0 indicates that the rule captures only a subset of the component, typically a clean core rather than the full region.

\subsection{Decision Boundary Proximity}
\label{sec:results-margin}

A natural hypothesis is that high-variance observations arise mainly near the decision boundary, where the ensemble mean prediction $\bar{p}_i$ is close to $0.5$. We test this by computing the margin $m_i = |\bar{p}_i - 0.5|$ and relating it to pointwise predictive variance.

Table~\ref{tab:margin_summary} shows a clear cross-dataset contrast. On Adult and German Credit, variance and margin are negatively correlated, indicating that high-variance observations tend to lie closer to the decision boundary. This pattern is especially pronounced in rank terms: the mean Spearman correlation is $-0.89$ on Adult and $-0.70$ on German Credit. The same conclusion is reflected in the HH vs.\ non-HH comparison, where HH points have substantially smaller margins on both datasets ($0.22$ vs.\ $0.37$ on Adult and $0.16$ vs.\ $0.26$ on German Credit). Thus, on these datasets, predictive multiplicity is at least partly associated with boundary ambiguity.

By contrast, COMPAS shows almost no relationship between variance and boundary proximity. The mean Pearson correlation is $-0.04 \pm 0.09$ and the mean Spearman correlation is $-0.07 \pm 0.18$, both close to zero. HH points do not exhibit smaller margins than non-HH points; if anything, their mean margin is slightly larger ($0.19$ vs.\ $0.18$). This indicates that multiplicity on COMPAS is not primarily a boundary phenomenon, but instead reflects structural disagreement among near-optimal models.

The Mann--Whitney U test supports the same interpretation. On Adult, the alternative hypothesis that HH points have lower margins than non-HH points is significant in all runs (mean $p \approx 3.5 \times 10^{-22}$). On German Credit, the effect is weaker but still present, with significance in $57\%$ of runs. On COMPAS, by contrast, the test is significant in only $30\%$ of runs and the mean p-value is $0.59$, providing no evidence that HH points systematically lie closer to the decision boundary.

Taken together, these results suggest that predictive multiplicity arises from different mechanisms across datasets. On Adult and German Credit, high-multiplicity regions are more closely linked to boundary uncertainty. On COMPAS, however, multiplicity is largely decoupled from decision-boundary proximity, reinforcing the interpretation that its hotspot structure is driven mainly by structural disagreement among near-optimal models rather than by intrinsic classification ambiguity.

\subsection{Summary of Results}

The experimental results show that predictive multiplicity is not merely a global
disagreement summary, but a structured phenomenon with spatial, model-dependent, and
interpretable components. Across datasets, model families, and robustness checks, several
main patterns emerge.

\begin{enumerate}

\item \textbf{Spatial structure:} Predictive multiplicity is spatially clustered in feature
space. Global Moran's $I$ is positive and statistically significant under the permutation
null in all outer runs for COMPAS, German Credit, and Adult. This shows that
disagreement is not randomly distributed over the test set, but systematically aligned
with the geometry of the feature space.

\item \textbf{Magnitude and concentration differ across datasets:} Disagreement magnitude
and spatial concentration are not the same. German Credit exhibits the largest overall
predictive variance and conflict, whereas COMPAS shows the clearest spatial
concentration of high-multiplicity observations. Adult has weaker Moran's $I$ but a
larger absolute number of HH observations, indicating broader and more diffuse hotspot
structure.

\item \textbf{Region-level stability despite point-level instability:} Exact HH membership varies across runs, but the current fixed-test analysis shows moderate rather than negligible pointwise overlap on COMPAS, with mean pairwise Jaccard similarity around 0.34. Connected-component analyses further show that HH observations often form contiguous regions in the feature-space graph. Predictive multiplicity should therefore be interpreted as a regional phenomenon with partially recurring structure, while avoiding the stronger claim that region-level hotspots are substantially more stable than pointwise HH labels.

\item \textbf{Complementary disagreement mechanisms:} Soft prediction variance and hard
conflict ratio capture distinct forms of multiplicity. Their overlap is limited
(Jaccard $\approx 0.06$ on COMPAS), and the quadrant analysis shows that observations
can exhibit high probability spread without label flips, or high threshold sensitivity
without especially large probability spread. Both metrics are therefore needed for a
complete description of predictive disagreement.

\item \textbf{Model family is a major driver:} Model family explains a substantial share of predictive variance, and this share increases inside hotspot regions on COMPAS and Adult. German Credit also shows high family importance, but its HH-specific estimate is less stable because very few HH points are detected. In the
Rashomon set, family importance rises from about $0.41$ to $0.52$ on COMPAS and
from about $0.71$ to $0.79$ on Adult when moving from all observations to HH regions.
German Credit also shows high family importance, although HH-specific estimates are
less stable because very few HH points are detected.

\item \textbf{Within-family hyperparameters add structured variation:} Within model families, disagreement is not diffuse across all hyperparameters. A small
number of hyperparameters repeatedly explain much of the remaining variation, such as
regularization strength $C$ for logistic regression, the number of neighbors for kNN,
learning-rate and tree-depth parameters for GBM, and learning-rate initialization or
architecture choices for MLPs.

\item \textbf{Robustness to analytical choices:} The main spatial conclusions are robust to
reasonable changes in the analysis design. Increasing Rashomon set size $K$ mainly
increases the magnitude of disagreement, while the spatial pattern stabilizes after
moderate values of $K$. Increasing the neighborhood size $k$ changes the smoothness
and extent of detected regions, but does not remove positive spatial autocorrelation.
Post-hoc calibration and alternative graph constructions likewise alter exact HH masks
without eliminating the underlying clustering signal.

\item \textbf{Validation on synthetic data:} On synthetic datasets with known ambiguous
regions, the pipeline recovers the intended instability regions with high precision and
substantial recall. In the single-island design, LISA HH detection achieves precision
$0.97$, recall $0.80$, and Jaccard overlap $0.78$. In the three-islands design, it achieves
precision $0.96$, recall $0.88$, and Jaccard overlap $0.85$. This supports the validity of
the spatial pipeline under controlled ground-truth conditions.

\item \textbf{Interpretability of hotspot regions:} High-multiplicity regions can be partly
described by compact rules in the original feature space. Global HH rules show a strong
support--purity trade-off, whereas component-level rules provide cleaner regional
descriptions. On COMPAS, retained component-level rules repeatedly involve
\texttt{priors\_count} and age, with representative component rules reaching very high
lift and a median best-rule lift of $26.7$ across retained components.

\item \textbf{Different mechanisms across datasets:} Predictive multiplicity is not driven
by the same mechanism everywhere. On Adult and German Credit, high variance is
strongly associated with proximity to the decision boundary, suggesting a link to
boundary ambiguity. On COMPAS, the correlation between variance and margin is close
to zero ($r \approx -0.04$), indicating that hotspot structure is better explained by
structural disagreement among near-optimal models than by ordinary boundary
uncertainty.

\item \textbf{Fairness implications are exploratory:} Subgroup exposure analysis on COMPAS suggests that multiplicity exposure is unevenly distributed across demographic groups. In the current stratified permutation analysis, race-based HH exposure disparities are statistically significant, whereas sex-based disparities are not. Because this analysis is observational and some subgroup sizes are small, the result should be interpreted as an exploratory auditing signal rather than as evidence of a causal disparity.

\end{enumerate}

Overall, the findings support the central claim of this thesis: predictive multiplicity is a
localized and structured property of the interaction between data geometry, model family,
and hyperparameter choice. It concentrates in identifiable regions of feature space, remains
visible under several robustness checks, and can be partially characterized by interpretable
rules. This makes spatial multiplicity analysis a useful diagnostic for identifying
predictions whose outputs are especially contingent on the choice among near-optimal
models.
