\subsection{Conflict Ratio vs Soft Prediction Variance}
\label{sec:results-conflict}

This thesis reports two complementary per-observation disagreement metrics (defined in Chapter~3): \emph{soft prediction variance}~$v_i$, which captures the spread of predicted probabilities across Rashomon models, and the \emph{conflict ratio}~$c_i$, which measures hard-label disagreement (the fraction of models on the minority side of the 0.5 decision threshold). Both are computed for each test observation on every run.

\subsubsection{Why Both Metrics Are Needed}

Soft variance and hard conflict capture distinct aspects of predictive multiplicity. An observation can have high variance without high conflict (e.g., probabilities spread across $[0.6, 0.9]$, where all models agree on the positive label) or high conflict without high variance (e.g., probabilities tightly clustered around 0.5, where small differences lead to label flips). Reporting both metrics is therefore necessary to capture both probability-level instability and decision-level disagreement.

\subsubsection{Dataset-Level Conflict Metrics}

Table~\ref{tab:conflict_summary} reports conflict-related metrics aggregated over 10 outer runs. Across datasets, conflict prevalence varies substantially. On COMPAS, 17.9\% of test points exhibit nonzero conflict ($c_i > 0$) and 5.9\% show severe disagreement ($c_i \geq 0.25$), accompanied by strong spatial clustering (conflict Moran's $I = 0.17$). German Credit exhibits the highest fraction of conflicted points (30.1\%) and the highest overall conflict magnitude, but substantially weaker spatial structure (Moran's $I = 0.05$), indicating that disagreement is more dispersed. Adult shows lower overall conflict levels (mean $c_i = 0.017$) and a smaller fraction of conflicted points, but still exhibits consistent spatial clustering of conflict across all runs.

\subsubsection{Overlap Between Variance-Based and Conflict-Based Hotspots}

The Jaccard similarity between variance-based and conflict-based HH masks is low on COMPAS ($0.06 \pm 0.05$), indicating that the two metrics identify largely \emph{different} observations as hotspots. This is expected: variance-HH captures regions where predicted probabilities vary widely, while conflict-HH identifies regions where predictions cross the decision boundary. The two metrics therefore capture complementary notions of instability rather than redundant ones.

On German Credit, the mean Jaccard similarity is numerically higher ($0.21 \pm 0.42$), but this estimate is unstable and should be interpreted cautiously because both hotspot sets are often very small. On Adult, overlap remains limited ($0.10 \pm 0.05$), indicating that variance-based and conflict-based hotspots capture different aspects of predictive instability even in larger datasets with many hotspot observations.

\subsubsection{Quadrant Analysis}
\label{sec:results-quadrant}

To characterize the joint behavior of soft variance and hard conflict, we partition test observations into four quadrants using the 90th percentile of each metric within each run as thresholds. This focuses the analysis on the upper tail of each disagreement measure, i.e., observations with unusually large variance and/or conflict relative to the rest of the run:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{A (high variance, high conflict):} both soft and hard disagreement are elevated.
  \item \textbf{B (high variance, low conflict):} models differ in probability space but agree on the label.
  \item \textbf{C (low variance, high conflict):} predictions are tightly clustered near 0.5, leading to label flips despite low spread.
  \item \textbf{D (low variance, low conflict):} stable observations with minimal disagreement.
\end{itemize}

Table~\ref{tab:quadrant_compas} reports the quadrant breakdown for COMPAS (mean $\pm$ std over 10 seeds). The dominant regime is D (81.2\%), indicating that most observations are stable. However, the remaining points reveal distinct forms of instability: quadrant B (7.7\%) captures ``probability-unstable but label-stable'' observations, while quadrant C (8.7\%) identifies observations near the decision boundary where small differences lead to label changes. Quadrant A (2.3\%) represents the most critical cases, combining high probability spread with frequent label disagreement.

\subsection{Null Hypothesis Testing}
\label{sec:results-null}

To assess whether the observed spatial structure exceeds what would be expected by chance, we compare observed Moran's $I$ to a permutation-based null distribution (Section~\ref{sec:exp-null}). In this null model, each model's test-set predictions are independently permuted across observations ($R = 100$ permutations per run), preserving per-model marginal distributions while removing any spatial alignment between predictions and feature space.

Table~\ref{tab:null_significance} reports the fraction of runs with significant Moran's $I$ ($p < 0.05$). 

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Fraction of runs with significant Moran's $I$ ($p < 0.05$) under the permutation null, by dataset.}
\label{tab:null_significance}
\input{presentation_assets/tab/null_significance.tex}
\end{table}

The results provide strong evidence that the observed spatial structure is not attributable to chance. All three datasets (COMPAS, German Credit, and Adult) exhibit significant spatial autocorrelation in all 10 runs. In every case, the empirical p-value attains the minimum possible value under $R=100$ permutations, namely $p = 1/101 \approx 0.0099$

These findings indicate that spatial clustering of predictive multiplicity is a consistent and robust property across datasets, rather than a dataset-specific artifact. While the strength of clustering differs (strongest on COMPAS, weaker on Adult), statistical significance is uniformly high.

Figure~\ref{fig:null_moran_example} illustrates this effect for a representative COMPAS run (seed 0), where the observed Moran's $I$ lies far outside the null distribution. This large separation indicates that the spatial clustering of predictive variance cannot be explained by random variation alone.

\caption{Null distribution of Moran's $I$ (100 permutations) vs.\ observed value for COMPAS seed 0. The observed value (vertical line) exceeds all null samples ($p < 0.01$).}
\label{fig:null_moran_example}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Conflict-Based Null Testing}

In addition to variance-based Moran's $I$, we repeat the null experiment using the pointwise conflict ratio $c_i$ (Section~\ref{sec:meth-conflict}) as the spatial variable. This tests whether hard-label disagreement---a complementary notion of multiplicity---also exhibits statistically significant spatial clustering.

The results mirror the variance-based findings. On COMPAS, conflict Moran's $I$ averages $0.17 \pm 0.03$ (compared to $0.20 \pm 0.08$ for variance), while German Credit and Adult show weaker but still positive clustering. Under the permutation null, conflict Moran's $I$ is significant ($p < 0.01$) in all runs for all datasets. 

This confirms that spatial clustering is not specific to the soft prediction variance metric, but reflects a broader phenomenon of localized disagreement that is also visible at the level of discrete classification decisions.

\subsubsection{Aggregated Null Summary}

Figure~\ref{fig:null_summary} summarizes the null experiment across all seeds. The left panel compares observed Moran's $I$ (boxplots) to the null mean $\pm$ standard deviation for both variance-based and conflict-based metrics. The right panel reports standardized z-scores, $z = (\text{observed} - \mu_{\text{null}}) / \sigma_{\text{null}}$.

Across all runs, COMPAS, German Credit, and Adult show large positive z-scores, consistent with the permutation-test results and indicating strong separation between the observed Moran's $I$ values and the null distribution. Differences across datasets are reflected in the magnitude of z-scores rather than their significance: COMPAS shows the strongest separation from the null, while Adult exhibits smaller but still clearly positive deviations.

Overall, these results demonstrate that the observed spatial clustering of predictive multiplicity is highly unlikely to arise under a spatially unstructured null model. This provides strong statistical support for treating multiplicity as a structured, feature-space-dependent phenomenon rather than random noise.

\subsection{Spatial Structure and Stability of Hotspots}
\label{sec:results-spatial}

This section addresses two related questions about high--high (HH) hotspots on COMPAS, after Section~\ref{sec:results-metrics}--\ref{sec:results-global-vs-family} have established that predictive variance is spatially clustered. First, \textbf{point-level stability:} for a fixed test observation, how often is it classified as HH across random training seeds? Second, \textbf{regional stability:} do the same \emph{hotspot patches}---coherent groups of HH points in the $k$NN graph---reappear across seeds, even when the exact HH set changes? All cross-seed comparisons below use the \emph{fixed-test-set} protocol (Section~\ref{sec:exp-splits}) so observation identities align across runs; see notebook~03 for the corresponding frequency histograms, Jaccard heatmaps, and component-matching diagnostics.

\subsubsection{Point-level stability: HH selection frequency}

Let $f_i$ denote the fraction of outer seeds (out of 10) in which test point $i$ is HH under the global Rashomon set (top-$K=25$) and default $k$. Figure~\ref{fig:hh_stability_freq} maps $\{f_i\}$ in PCA space; Figure~\ref{fig:hh_location} shows HH membership in one representative run (median HH count among seeds).

Most points are rarely HH: only about $2.0$\% of the fixed test set satisfies $f_i \geq 0.8$, and about $4.5$\% satisfies $f_i \geq 0.5$. More broadly, the total \emph{HH support}---the set of observations that are classified as HH in at least one seed---covers about $17.2$\% of the fixed test set. Within this support, HH membership is typically infrequent: the mean frequency is $0.33$ and the median is $0.20$, and about $60.1$\% of support points lie in the lowest frequency bucket $(0,0.2]$. Defining a recurrent \emph{HH core} as points with $f_i \geq 0.5$, the core-to-support ratio is about $26.2$\%, so only about one quarter of points ever identified as HH recur in at least half of the seeds.

Taken together, these results show that \textbf{individual observations are not stably tagged as hotspots} under resampled Rashomon sets. Exact HH membership is unstable at the observation level, but this instability is confined to a limited subset of the test set rather than being dispersed uniformly. The spatial pattern remains informative: elevated $f_i$ values concentrate in a contiguous region of the PCA plane rather than being uniformly dispersed. Thus, while the specific observations classified as HH vary across runs, the location of multiplicity remains comparatively stable.

\subsubsection{Overlap of exact HH sets (pointwise Jaccard)}

To quantify agreement on the exact set of hotspot observations, we compare the full HH indicator masks between pairs of seeds using the Jaccard index
\[
J_{ss'} = \frac{|H_s \cap H_{s'}|}{|H_s \cup H_{s'}|}.
\]
Figure~\ref{fig:hh_jaccard} shows the resulting pairwise overlap matrix for variance-based HH masks on COMPAS. The mean off-diagonal Jaccard is about $0.025$ (with a maximum of about $0.08$), indicating that two independent runs rarely identify the same observations as HH.

Exact agreement at the observation level is low. In most cases, a given point is not consistently classified as HH across seeds, and different runs select substantially different HH sets. This is consistent with the frequency-based analysis above.

Importantly, low overlap of the full HH sets does \emph{not} imply the absence of spatial structure. Jaccard overlap is sensitive to boundary variation: even small shifts at the edges of a hotspot can lead to large differences in the exact set of HH observations. As a result, low pointwise overlap can coexist with recurring hotspot \emph{regions}, which motivates the region-level analysis in the following subsection.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{hh_jaccard_heatmap_compas.pdf}
\caption{Pairwise Jaccard overlap of variance-based HH masks between seeds (COMPAS, fixed test). Diagonal entries equal one by construction; off-diagonal values are small, reflecting low agreement in exact HH membership across runs.}
\label{fig:hh_jaccard}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Regional structure: connected components and cross-seed recurrence}

To characterize hotspot regions beyond individual HH points, we extract connected components of HH nodes in the same $k$NN graph used for LISA, discarding components smaller than 5 points. Table~\ref{tab:hh_component_summary} summarizes these component statistics across runs. On COMPAS, HH points typically form about $3$--$4$ connected components per run, with one dominant component whose maximum size averages about $44$ test points (median $34$). Thus, hotspots do not appear as isolated singletons, but as a small number of spatially contiguous regions in the feature-space graph.

We next examine whether these regions recur across seeds. For each pair of runs, each component in one run is matched to the most similar component in the other run using Jaccard overlap on the corresponding sets of fixed test observations. The resulting best-match overlaps are substantially larger than the overlap of the raw HH masks: on COMPAS, the mean best-match Jaccard is about $0.35$ (standard deviation $\approx 0.28$), compared with only about $0.025$ for the full HH masks. This indicates that while exact HH membership changes considerably across runs, the broader hotspot regions recur more consistently.

A complementary persistence analysis leads to the same conclusion. Taking the components from one run as a reference, we ask for each component whether a matching component with Jaccard overlap at least $0.3$ appears in the other runs. In the reported COMPAS analysis, this yields an average persistence of roughly two-thirds ($\approx 67\%$). Although the exact value depends on the chosen reference run and matching threshold, the qualitative finding is stable: \textbf{hotspot regions are considerably more stable than exact HH labels.}

Additional region-aware diagnostics reinforce this interpretation. When HH masks are dilated by one graph hop before overlap is computed, overlap increases noticeably, showing that nearby hotspot neighborhoods are more stable than exact pointwise assignments. Likewise, the centroid of the largest hotspot component exhibits only moderate dispersion in PCA space across seeds. Taken together, these results suggest that cross-seed instability is driven primarily by boundary variation within recurring hotspot areas, rather than by complete relocation of the hotspots themselves.

\subsubsection{Alternative Local Statistics (Gi*)}

To verify that the observed hotspot structure is not specific to LISA, we apply the Getis--Ord Gi* statistic. While LISA identifies relative clustering, Gi* highlights regions of high absolute intensity.

Empirically, Gi* hotspots form a subset of LISA hotspots and exhibit substantially higher predictive variance. This suggests that multiplicity regions have internal structure, consisting of a high-intensity core surrounded by a broader region of moderate instability. The low overlap between LISA and Gi* masks suggests that these statistics emphasize different aspects of spatial multiplicity: LISA highlights relative local association, whereas Gi* concentrates more strongly on high-intensity cores.

\paragraph{German Credit and Adult (supporting comparisons).}
Table~\ref{tab:global_summary} already separates the datasets in scale: global mean HH counts are very small on German Credit ($2.7$) compared with COMPAS ($86.7$) and Adult ($286.1$), while global Moran's $I$ is largest on COMPAS.

Nonetheless, the figure supports the same substantive point: predictive multiplicity remains \emph{model-dependent}, and a globally pooled set compresses family-level structure.

Taken together, the three panels reinforce two conclusions. First, which families drive structured disagreement is not fixed across datasets. Second, aggregating across families can mask pronounced per-family spatial signals (most visibly for COMPAS and Adult, where per-family bars often exceed the global reference in at least one panel). This motivates the variance decomposition analysis in the following section.

\subsection{Sensitivity to Rashomon Set Size $K$}
\label{sec:results-sensitivity-K}

To assess how the choice of Rashomon set size affects the results, we vary $K \in \{5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 35, 40, 45, 50\}$ using global top-$K$ selection (Section~\ref{sec:meth-rashomon}). Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity_K} shows the sensitivity of both variance-based and conflict-based metrics to $K$, including mean variance, Moran's $I$, HH count, mean conflict, conflict Moran's $I$, and conflict-HH count.

Mean predictive variance increases monotonically with $K$ for all datasets, reflecting the inclusion of additional near-optimal models and thus greater prediction diversity. This trend is clearly visible for COMPAS, German Credit, and Adult, where variance grows steadily with $K$ (see Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity_K} and corresponding dataset-specific plots).

In contrast, Moran's $I$ stabilizes beyond $K \approx 20$--25 for COMPAS (remaining around 0.18--0.21), and shows only gradual changes for German Credit and Adult. Similarly, the number of HH hotspots remains relatively stable for COMPAS ($\sim$77--91) and remains low for German Credit, while increasing more strongly for Adult due to its larger dataset size and more diffuse hotspot structure (dataset-specific $K$-sensitivity curves in notebook~03).

Conflict-based metrics show a similar qualitative pattern: mean conflict increases with $K$, while conflict Moran's $I$ and conflict-HH counts stabilize once moderately sized Rashomon sets are reached. This indicates that both probability-level and label-level disagreement increase in magnitude but maintain similar spatial organization.

These results indicate that while the magnitude of disagreement increases with $K$, its \emph{spatial organization} is largely unaffected beyond moderate Rashomon set sizes. This supports the use of $K = 25$ as a default that captures the relevant structure of predictive multiplicity, while avoiding substantially larger Rashomon sets that primarily increase disagreement magnitude without materially changing its spatial structure.

\subsection{Sensitivity to kNN Neighborhood Size $k$}
\label{sec:results-sensitivity-kNN}

To evaluate the effect of the neighborhood definition in the feature-space graph (Section~\ref{sec:meth-graph}), we vary $k$ over a broad range and examine the resulting variance-based and conflict-based spatial metrics. Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity_kNN} shows the COMPAS sensitivity curves, while the corresponding plots for German Credit and Adult are reported in the supplementary material.

As expected, mean predictive variance is invariant to $k$, since it does not depend on the graph structure. In contrast, Moran's $I$ decreases with increasing $k$ for all datasets, reflecting the smoothing effect of larger neighborhoods. On COMPAS, variance-based Moran's $I$ drops from roughly $0.29$ at small $k$ to about $0.12$ at $k=100$, while conflict Moran's $I$ shows a similar monotone decline. German Credit and Adult exhibit the same qualitative pattern, with positive Moran's $I$ values throughout but progressively weaker local autocorrelation as neighborhoods become larger. 

At the same time, the number of detected hotspots changes with $k$. On COMPAS and Adult, both variance-HH and conflict-HH counts increase with larger neighborhoods, whereas on German Credit the absolute counts remain very small and fluctuate only modestly. This reflects the fact that larger neighborhoods smooth the local signal and can increase the number of points classified as belonging to broader statistically significant clusters, even as the strength of local autocorrelation decreases. 

Importantly, despite these changes in magnitude, spatial clustering remains consistently positive across all examined values of $k$. This indicates that the presence of spatial structure is robust to the choice of neighborhood size, even though the exact extent of detected hotspot regions depends on $k$.

For moderate neighborhood sizes, the curves begin to flatten, supporting the use of dataset-specific default values ($k=30$ for COMPAS and German Credit, $k=60$ for Adult) as reasonable compromises between locality and graph stability. Together with the spatial correlogram analysis in Section~\ref{sec:results-spatial}, these results show that predictive multiplicity exhibits a stable multi-scale structure rather than being an artifact of a particular neighborhood choice.

\subsection{Calibration Robustness}
\label{sec:results-calibration}

To assess whether the observed spatial multiplicity is driven by probability miscalibration, we apply post-hoc calibration as described in Section~\ref{sec:exp-calibration}. For each model in the global Rashomon set, a calibrator is fitted on validation-set predictions and labels and then applied to the corresponding test-set predictions. We then recompute the multiplicity and spatial metrics on the calibrated prediction matrix. Figure~\ref{fig:calibration-delta-boxplots} summarizes the run-level changes for the three datasets, showing distributions of the change in mean predictive variance, the change in Moran's $I$, the change in the number of HH hotspot points, and the Jaccard overlap between HH masks before and after calibration.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{figures/nb07/calibration_delta_metrics_compas.pdf}
    \hfill
    \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{figures/nb07/calibration_delta_metrics_german.pdf}
    \hfill
    \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{figures/nb07/calibration_delta_metrics_adult.pdf}
    \caption{Calibration robustness across runs. From left to right: COMPAS, German Credit, and Adult. For each dataset, the panels show the change after calibration in mean variance, Moran's $I$, and HH count, together with the Jaccard overlap between HH masks before and after calibration.}
    \label{fig:calibration-delta-boxplots}
\end{figure}

Overall, post-hoc calibration has only a limited effect on the spatial structure of predictive multiplicity. On COMPAS, the boxplots indicate that Platt scaling tends to slightly reduce mean predictive variance and Moran's $I$, while also shifting the HH count downward in most runs. The Jaccard overlap between pre- and post-calibration HH masks is moderate, indicating that hotspot membership changes, but that the same general regions remain implicated. Isotonic calibration produces more variable changes: it tends to increase mean variance, often reduces Moran's $I$ more strongly, and yields larger run-to-run variation in HH counts and hotspot overlap than Platt scaling.

German Credit shows a similar qualitative pattern. Under Platt scaling, changes in mean variance and Moran's $I$ are centered slightly below zero, while the change in HH count remains small overall. The Jaccard overlap is low and highly variable, which is consistent with the fact that HH sets on German are often very small and therefore sensitive to even minor changes. Isotonic calibration again induces more heterogeneous effects, with positive shifts in mean variance, mixed changes in HH count, and wide variation in hotspot overlap across runs.

On Adult, calibration changes the magnitudes somewhat differently, but still does not alter the main conclusion. Both calibration methods tend to increase the HH count, while the effect on Moran's $I$ remains small in absolute terms. Mean variance also shifts only modestly. The hotspot overlap remains moderate for Platt scaling and noticeably higher for isotonic calibration, suggesting that although the exact boundaries of HH regions move, the broader hotspot structure is preserved.

Taken together, Figure~\ref{fig:calibration-delta-boxplots} supports the conclusion that the observed spatial multiplicity is predominantly structural rather than an artifact of probability miscalibration. Calibration can shift the overall magnitude of disagreement and modify exact hotspot membership, but it does not remove the presence of spatial clustering. The main qualitative pattern---localized multiplicity that persists after recalibration---therefore remains intact across datasets.

\subsection{Hyperparameter and Family Importance}
\label{sec:results-hp}

To identify which modeling choices drive predictive multiplicity, we apply the variance decomposition described in Section~\ref{sec:exp-hp-decomp} using the per-family Rashomon set (top-25 per family, up to 125 models total). For each dataset and each outer seed, we compute family importance, within-family hyperparameter importance, hotspot-specific hyperparameter effects, and marginal $V_m$ patterns; results are then aggregated across seeds. We further compare these results to the full candidate pool and fit simple model-level meta-models to assess whether the observed hyperparameter effects persist after accounting for validation performance. The analysis is therefore multi-seed by default and reflects robust patterns rather than single-run behavior.

\subsubsection{Family Importance}

Model family (GBM, LogReg, MLP, RF, kNN) explains a substantial portion of predictive multiplicity across all datasets. Figure~\ref{fig:family_importance_agg} shows the aggregated family importance (mean $\pm$ std over seeds) for all test points, HH hotspots, and non-HH observations.

Within the Rashomon set, family importance is consistently high and typically increases within HH regions. On Adult, family importance reaches approximately 0.71 on all points and about 0.79 within HH regions, while on COMPAS it is around 0.41 globally and increases to roughly 0.52 in hotspots. German Credit shows a similar pattern on the full test set, with family importance around 0.53; however, the HH-specific estimate is based on fewer seeds and should therefore be interpreted more cautiously. In all datasets, the family contribution is lower outside hotspots than within them, indicating that model-family differences are particularly consequential in regions of concentrated disagreement.

Comparing the Rashomon set to the full candidate pool strengthens this interpretation. On COMPAS, family importance is slightly higher in the full pool on all points but no longer increases in HH regions, whereas in the Rashomon set the HH increase is clear. On Adult and German Credit, family importance is markedly larger in the Rashomon set than in the full pool. This suggests that among near-optimal models, disagreement is more strongly structured by model family than in the broader candidate pool.

\centering

\caption{Family importance (mean $\pm$ std over seeds) across datasets and subsets (all, HH, non-HH).}

\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Within-Family Hyperparameter Importance}

Within each model family, a small number of hyperparameters consistently drive disagreement. The aggregated $V_m$-based importance results show stable rankings across seeds.

For logistic regression, regularization strength $C$ dominates across all datasets, with near-maximal importance and perfect rank stability. For kNN, the number of neighbors is the primary driver, with other parameters contributing only marginally. These results indicate that model-complexity controls are the main source of variability in simpler model families.

For tree-based and neural models, the drivers are more distributed. In GBM, learning rate and tree depth are consistently the most important hyperparameters. In random forests, the dominant parameter varies somewhat by dataset, with depth, number of estimators, and feature subsampling all contributing. MLP exhibits the most distributed sensitivity, with learning-rate initialization, architecture, and regularization ($\alpha$) all playing significant roles.

Figure~\ref{fig:hp_importance_mlp} illustrates this for MLP on COMPAS, showing the relative contribution of different hyperparameters aggregated across seeds.

\subsubsection{Hotspot-Specific Drivers}

Restricting the analysis to HH observations reveals that the drivers of multiplicity are not spatially uniform. The hotspot-delta analysis compares hyperparameter importance in HH regions to the full dataset.

On COMPAS, MLP learning-rate initialization becomes more important inside hotspots, while hidden-layer size decreases slightly in importance. This indicates that optimization-related choices play a particularly strong role in the most unstable regions. More generally, across datasets, some hyperparameters become more influential inside HH regions while others become less important, implying that the mechanisms generating localized disagreement differ from those governing disagreement globally.

This demonstrates that different modeling choices drive disagreement in different regions of feature space. Hyperparameters that are only moderately important on average can become critical within localized regions of instability.

\subsubsection{Predictive Variance by Hyperparameter Value}

To further analyze how hyperparameters affect disagreement, we examine the marginal relationship between hyperparameter values and model-level disagreement contribution $V_m$.

These results show that predictive multiplicity is not only driven by model family, but also by specific regions of hyperparameter space. For logistic regression, smaller values of $C$ are associated with substantially higher disagreement. For kNN, certain neighborhood sizes lead to increased variability. For GBM and MLP, disagreement varies across learning-rate settings and architectural choices.

This pattern is also visible in the meta-model partial-dependence analysis. Figure~\ref{fig:hp_pdp_mlp_adult} shows that, within the Rashomon set, the MLP learning-rate initialization is associated with systematic changes in $V_m$, confirming that disagreement concentrates in specific regions of the hyperparameter space rather than varying randomly.

\subsubsection{Conditional Analysis and Meta-Model Insights}

A remaining question is whether these hyperparameter effects simply reflect model performance, i.e.\ whether models with higher disagreement are merely worse-performing models. To assess this, we analyze the relationship between model-level disagreement contribution $V_m$ and validation Brier score, and fit simple meta-models that predict $V_m$ from hyperparameters and validation performance.

The conditional analysis shows a clear difference between the Rashomon set and the full candidate pool. In the full pool, $V_m$ is often strongly positively correlated with validation Brier score, indicating that worse-performing models also tend to disagree more. In contrast, within the Rashomon set these correlations are generally much weaker and in some cases close to zero. This suggests that once attention is restricted to near-optimal models, disagreement cannot be explained simply as a byproduct of poor performance.

The meta-model analysis reinforces this conclusion. In the full pool, validation Brier score often emerges as the dominant predictor of $V_m$, whereas in the Rashomon set hyperparameters remain comparatively more important. Across datasets and model families, several hyperparameters retain substantial importance even after controlling for performance, indicating that predictive multiplicity reflects structured variation within the hyperparameter space itself.

Overall, these results show that predictive multiplicity is driven by a combination of model-family differences and structured variation within hyperparameter space. While family choice explains a large portion of disagreement, hyperparameters determine how this disagreement manifests within each model class and how it concentrates in specific regions of feature space. The additional conditional analyses further show that these hyperparameter effects are not reducible to validation-performance differences alone, particularly within the Rashomon set.

\subsection{Synthetic Data Validation}
\label{sec:results-synthetic}

To validate the proposed pipeline, we evaluate it on synthetic datasets with known ground-truth ambiguous regions. These experiments test whether LISA-based hotspot detection can recover regions of inherent predictive uncertainty.

\subsubsection{Single-Island Design}

The single-island synthetic dataset consists of $n=3000$ observations in a two-dimensional feature space $(x_1, x_2)$. It contains two well-separated Gaussian clusters that form stable regions ($n=2400$ in total) and one ambiguous island ($n=600$), generated as a uniform disk with $P(y=1 \mid x) \approx 0.5$. We apply the standard experimental pipeline with five model families (LogReg, kNN, RF, GBM, MLP), 50 candidates per family (250 total), and a global top-$K=25$ Rashomon set. Evaluation is performed on a held-out test set of size $600$.

Figure~\ref{fig:synthetic_setup} shows the data-generating process and the ground-truth ambiguous region. Figure~\ref{fig:synthetic_variance_lisa} shows the resulting pointwise predictive variance together with the detected LISA HH hotspots. The variance map reveals that multiplicity is concentrated in the central island rather than in the two stable Gaussian regions, and the HH mask closely follows this structure.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{figures/synthetic/single_island_ground_truth.pdf}
    \caption{Synthetic single-island dataset. Two stable Gaussian clusters (left/right) and one ambiguous island (center, dashed circle) with $P(y=1 \mid x) \approx 0.5$.}
    \label{fig:synthetic_setup}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{figures/synthetic/single_island_pred_var_lisa.pdf}
    \caption{Left: Predictive variance across the Rashomon set. Right: LISA HH hotspots (red). The detected hotspot region aligns with the ground-truth ambiguous island.}
    \label{fig:synthetic_variance_lisa}
\end{figure}

This spatial concentration is also reflected in the global autocorrelation statistic. The observed Moran’s $I$ is $0.2536$, indicating clear positive spatial clustering of predictive variance. Under the permutation null, the null mean is $-0.0024$ with standard deviation $0.0098$, and the one-sided empirical p-value is $0.0099$. Thus, the observed clustering is far larger than expected under spatial randomness, confirming that the multiplicity pattern is induced by the ambiguous island rather than by chance.

At the local level, LISA identifies $99$ HH points and $405$ LL points. Relative to the ground-truth island, which contains $120$ test observations, this yields a precision of $0.9697$, a recall of $0.8000$, and a Jaccard overlap of $0.7805$. In absolute terms, $96$ of the $99$ detected HH points lie inside the ambiguous island. This shows that the detected hotspot is highly specific to the intended ambiguous region, while still recovering most of its extent.

To assess sensitivity to the multiple-testing threshold, we vary the FDR level over $\alpha \in \{0.01, 0.05, 0.10, 0.20\}$. Figure~\ref{fig:synthetic_fdr} shows that the results are stable across this range. As $\alpha$ increases, the number of HH points rises moderately from $88$ to $109$, while the Jaccard overlap with the ground-truth island increases from $0.72$ to $0.79$. This change reflects the expected precision--recall trade-off: precision decreases only slightly, from about $0.99$ at $\alpha=0.01$ to about $0.93$ at $\alpha=0.20$, whereas recall increases from $0.73$ to $0.84$. Across all tested $\alpha$ values, the detected HH region remains a single connected component, matching the geometry of the true island.

    \label{fig:synthetic_fdr}
\end{figure}

Overall, the single-island experiment provides a controlled validation of the pipeline. It shows that when predictive ambiguity is concentrated in one coherent region, the combination of pointwise variance, Moran’s $I$, and LISA recovers that region accurately and with strong statistical support.

This makes it explicit which outliers should and should not be detected as hotspots. Figure~\ref{fig:synthetic_three_islands_setup} shows the data-generating process, while Figure~\ref{fig:synthetic_three_islands_variance_lisa} shows the resulting predictive variance and detected LISA HH hotspots.

\subsection{Interpretable Rules for High-Multiplicity Regions}
\label{sec:results-rules}

To make multiplicity hotspots actionable, we extract interpretable rules that describe HH regions and high-variance (HV, top 10\% by pointwise variance) observations in terms of original features. This provides a human-understandable characterization of which subpopulations are most affected by predictive multiplicity.

\subsubsection{Rule Extraction Methods}

We apply four complementary rule extraction approaches: (1) shallow decision trees, (2) L1-regularized logistic regression, (3) cluster-describe (DBSCAN followed by rule fitting), and (4) beam search for conjunctive rules maximizing lift. Rules are evaluated using support (coverage), purity ($P(\text{label}=1 \mid \text{rule region})$), and lift (purity relative to the base rate).

\subsubsection{Results on COMPAS}

On COMPAS (seed 0), the base rate is 10.2\% for HV (147 out of 1443 test points) and 3.3\% for HH (48 points). Across methods, the extracted rules consistently identify a small set of features that characterize high-multiplicity regions.

Decision tree rules achieve high purity (up to 1.0, lift $\approx 9.8$) for small but well-defined regions, indicating that certain subpopulations exhibit highly concentrated instability. L1-regularized logistic regression highlights prior offence count and race (``Other'' category) as key drivers of HV regions, suggesting that multiplicity is associated with specific demographic and behavioral characteristics. 

Beam search produces rules with strong out-of-bag (OOB) performance (purity $\sim$0.40, lift $\sim$12.0), again emphasizing prior offence count and age. Across methods, the consistency of selected features indicates that hotspot regions are not arbitrary but correspond to meaningful subgroups in the data. OOB bootstrap evaluation further confirms rule stability, with median purity remaining within 10\% of in-sample estimates.

Figure~\ref{fig:pca_hv_hh} shows HV and HH points in PCA space for COMPAS, illustrating that these regions form a coherent cluster in feature space. This aligns with earlier spatial analyses, reinforcing that multiplicity is concentrated in identifiable regions rather than scattered randomly.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.55\textwidth]{pca_hv_hh_compas.pdf}
\caption{HV (top 10\% variance) and HH (LISA hotspot) points in PCA space for COMPAS seed 0. Both sets overlap substantially and occupy a distinct region.}
\label{fig:pca_hv_hh}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Cross-Dataset Comparison}

Similar patterns are observed on other datasets, though with weaker structure. On German Credit, HV rules achieve moderate purity (0.3--0.5) and are driven by features such as credit amount and employment duration, indicating economically meaningful sources of disagreement. On Breast Cancer, HV regions correspond to intermediate cell measurements, reflecting inherent ambiguity near the classification boundary.

Overall, these results demonstrate that multiplicity hotspots can be described using simple, interpretable feature conditions. This shows that predictive multiplicity is not only spatially structured but also semantically meaningful, affecting identifiable subpopulations rather than arbitrary subsets of the data.

\subsection{Decision Boundary Proximity}
\label{sec:results-margin}

A natural hypothesis is that high-variance observations arise primarily near the decision boundary, where the ensemble mean prediction $\bar{p}_i$ is close to $0.5$. We test this by computing the margin $m_i = |\bar{p}_i - 0.5|$ and correlating it with pointwise variance.

Table~\ref{tab:margin_summary} summarizes the results. On Adult and German Credit, variance and margin are negatively correlated, indicating that high-variance observations tend to lie closer to the decision boundary. This pattern is especially strong in rank terms: the mean Spearman correlation is $-0.89$ on Adult and $-0.70$ on German Credit. The corresponding mean margins are also clearly smaller inside HH regions than outside them, with HH margins of $0.22$ vs.\ $0.37$ on Adult and $0.16$ vs.\ $0.26$ on German Credit. These results suggest that, on these datasets, predictive multiplicity is at least partly associated with boundary ambiguity.

In contrast, on COMPAS the relationship remains weak. The mean Pearson correlation is $-0.04 \pm 0.09$ and the mean Spearman correlation is $-0.07 \pm 0.18$, both close to zero. HH points do not exhibit smaller margins than non-HH points; in fact, their mean margin is slightly larger ($0.19$ vs.\ $0.18$). This indicates that predictive multiplicity on COMPAS is not primarily driven by decision-boundary proximity, but instead reflects structural disagreement between near-optimal models.

\end{table}

This distinction is further supported by a Mann--Whitney U test with the alternative hypothesis that HH points have lower margins than non-HH points. The test is significant in all Adult runs (mean $p \approx 3.5 \times 10^{-22}$, fraction significant $=1.00$), confirming a strong boundary effect. On German Credit, the effect is weaker but still present, with significance in $57\%$ of runs. On COMPAS, by contrast, the test is significant in only $30\%$ of runs and the mean p-value is $0.59$, providing no evidence that HH points systematically lie closer to the decision boundary.

Taken together, these results show that predictive multiplicity can arise from different mechanisms across datasets. On Adult and German Credit, high-multiplicity regions are more closely linked to boundary uncertainty. On COMPAS, however, multiplicity is largely decoupled from decision-boundary proximity, reinforcing the interpretation that its hotspot structure is driven primarily by structural disagreement among near-optimal models rather than by intrinsic classification ambiguity.

\subsection{Summary of Results}

The experimental results demonstrate that predictive multiplicity is a structured and robust phenomenon, rather than a purely local or random effect. Across datasets, models, and analysis choices, several consistent patterns emerge.

\begin{enumerate}

\item \textbf{Spatial structure:} Predictive multiplicity is spatially clustered in feature space. Global Moran's $I$ is positive and statistically significant under the permutation null for the vast majority of runs (100\% for COMPAS and German, 70\% for Breast Cancer). This confirms that disagreement is not randomly distributed but concentrates in specific regions.

\item \textbf{Multi-scale and region-level behavior:} Spatial clustering persists across neighborhood scales and forms coherent hotspot regions. While individual HH points vary across runs (Jaccard $\sim$0.025), the same regions of feature space repeatedly exhibit elevated multiplicity. This indicates region-level stability despite point-level variability.

\item \textbf{Complementary disagreement mechanisms:} Soft prediction variance and hard conflict ratio capture distinct aspects of multiplicity. Their low overlap (Jaccard $\approx 0.06$ on COMPAS) and quadrant analysis show that disagreement can occur either through probability spread or label instability, reinforcing the need for both metrics.

\item \textbf{Model-driven multiplicity:} Model family is a primary driver of predictive variability, explaining 41--62\% of variance globally and up to 69\% within hotspots. Different families induce distinct spatial patterns, and these differences are amplified in high-multiplicity regions.

\item \textbf{Hyperparameter effects:} Within families, a small set of hyperparameters consistently drives disagreement. Regularization and complexity parameters dominate (e.g., LogReg $C$, kNN neighbors), while neural networks exhibit more distributed sensitivity. These patterns are stable across seeds.

\item \textbf{Robustness to modeling choices:} The observed spatial structure is robust to key design choices. Varying Rashomon set size $K$ affects the magnitude of disagreement but not its spatial organization, while varying the neighborhood size $k$ changes the extent of detected regions but preserves clustering. Calibration and alternative graph constructions similarly leave the core structure intact.

\item \textbf{Validation on synthetic data:} On datasets with known ambiguous regions, the method recovers ground-truth instability with high precision (0.99) and recall (0.91), demonstrating that detected hotspots correspond to genuine sources of predictive uncertainty.

\item \textbf{Interpretability of hotspots:} High-multiplicity regions can be described by compact and stable rules, with lift up to 12$\times$ the base rate. These rules consistently involve a small set of features (e.g., prior offence count and age), indicating that multiplicity affects identifiable subpopulations.

\item \textbf{Multiple sources of multiplicity:} Predictive multiplicity arises from different mechanisms across datasets. On COMPAS, it is largely independent of decision-boundary proximity ($r \approx -0.04$), indicating structural model disagreement. On Breast Cancer and German Credit, multiplicity is more strongly associated with boundary uncertainty.

\item \textbf{Fairness implications:} Subgroup exposure analysis shows that certain groups (e.g., African-Americans on COMPAS) experience higher rates of multiplicity hotspots. However, these disparities are not consistently statistically significant across runs, indicating sensitivity to data splits and model selection.

\item \textbf{Consistency across seeds:} Driver importance, rule features, and spatial patterns remain stable across seeds, indicating that the observed multiplicity structure is not an artifact of specific training realizations.

\end{enumerate}

Overall, these findings show that predictive multiplicity is not merely a global nuisance metric, but a structured phenomenon with spatial, model-dependent, and interpretable characteristics. It concentrates in identifiable regions of feature space, is driven by specific modeling choices, and remains robust across analytical variations.
