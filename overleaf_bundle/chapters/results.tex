\subsection{Conflict Ratio vs Soft Prediction Variance}
\label{sec:results-conflict}

This thesis reports two complementary per-observation disagreement metrics (defined in Sections~3.3--3.4): \emph{soft prediction variance}~$v_i$, which captures the spread of predicted probabilities across Rashomon models, and the \emph{conflict ratio}~$c_i$, which measures hard-label disagreement (the fraction of models on the minority side of the 0.5 decision threshold).  Both are computed for each test observation on every run.

\subsubsection{Why Both Metrics Are Needed}

Soft variance and hard conflict capture different aspects of multiplicity.  An observation can have high variance without high conflict (e.g., probabilities spread across $[0.6, 0.9]$---all models agree on the positive label) or high conflict without high variance (e.g., probabilities tightly clustered around 0.5 with a narrow but label-flipping spread).  Reporting both avoids the blind spot of either metric alone.

\subsubsection{Dataset-Level Conflict Metrics}

Table~\ref{tab:conflict_summary} reports conflict-related metrics aggregated over 10 outer runs.  On COMPAS, 17.9\% of test points exhibit nonzero conflict ($c_i > 0$) and 5.9\% have severe conflict ($c_i \geq 0.25$).  German Credit shows a higher fraction of conflicted points (30.1\%) but weaker spatial clustering of conflict (conflict Moran's $I = 0.05$).  Breast Cancer has the lowest conflict prevalence (5.4\% nonzero).

\subsubsection{Overlap Between Variance-Based and Conflict-Based Hotspots}

The Jaccard similarity between variance-HH and conflict-HH masks is low on COMPAS ($0.06 \pm 0.05$), indicating that the two metrics identify largely \emph{different} observations as hotspots.  This is expected: variance-HH captures regions where soft probabilities are spread, whereas conflict-HH captures regions where models straddle the decision threshold.  The two sets are complementary rather than redundant.  On German Credit, the Jaccard is higher ($0.21 \pm 0.42$) but driven by low absolute counts, and on Breast Cancer both masks are near-empty.

\subsubsection{Quadrant Analysis}
\label{sec:results-quadrant}

To characterize the joint distribution of soft variance and hard conflict, we partition test observations into four quadrants using the 90th percentile of each metric within each run as thresholds:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{A (high variance, high conflict):} both soft and hard disagreement are elevated.
  \item \textbf{B (high variance, low conflict):} models spread in probability space but agree on the label.
  \item \textbf{C (low variance, high conflict):} predictions are tightly clustered near 0.5, causing label flips despite low spread.
  \item \textbf{D (low variance, low conflict):} stable observations with no meaningful disagreement.
\end{itemize}

Table~\ref{tab:quadrant_compas} reports the quadrant breakdown for COMPAS (mean $\pm$ std over 10 seeds).  The dominant regime is D (81.2\%), confirming that most test observations are not affected by multiplicity.  Quadrant B (7.7\%) contains points with high soft variance but no label disagreement---these are ``probability-unstable but label-stable.''  Quadrant C (8.7\%) shows the converse: low spread but frequent label flips, identifying observations near the decision boundary where even small prediction differences change the classification.  Quadrant A (2.3\%) contains the most problematic observations: both high probability spread \emph{and} high label disagreement.

\subsection{Null Hypothesis Testing}
\label{sec:results-null}

To test whether the observed spatial structure exceeds what would be expected by chance, we compared observed Moran's $I$ to the null distribution described in Section~\ref{sec:exp-null}: each model's test-set predictions are independently permuted across observations ($R = 100$ permutations per run), preserving per-model marginal distributions while destroying spatial alignment.  Table~\ref{tab:null_significance} reports the fraction of runs with significant Moran's $I$ ($p < 0.05$).

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Fraction of runs with significant Moran's $I$ ($p < 0.05$) under the permutation null, by dataset.}
\label{tab:null_significance}
\input{presentation_assets/tab/null_significance.tex}
\end{table}

COMPAS and German Credit show significant spatial autocorrelation in all 10 runs.  In every case the observed Moran's $I$ exceeded all 100 null samples, yielding $p = 1/101 \approx 0.0099$---the resolution floor of the permutation test (see Section~\ref{sec:exp-null}).  Breast Cancer is significant in 70\% of runs; the 3 non-significant runs (seeds 3, 4, 9) correspond to seeds with lower overall multiplicity. Figure~\ref{fig:null_moran_example} shows an example null distribution for COMPAS (seed 0): the observed Moran's $I$ of 0.36 lies far above the null mean of $-0.001 \pm 0.006$.

\subsubsection{Conflict-Based Null Testing}

In addition to variance-based Moran's $I$, we repeat the null experiment using the pointwise conflict ratio $c_i$ (Section~\ref{sec:meth-conflict}) as the spatial variable.  This tests whether hard-label disagreement---a complementary measure to soft prediction variance---also exhibits significant spatial clustering.

On COMPAS, the observed conflict Moran's $I$ averages $0.17 \pm 0.03$ (cf.\ variance-based $0.20 \pm 0.08$); on German Credit, $0.05 \pm 0.03$ (cf.\ $0.10 \pm 0.04$).  Under the same permutation null (independently permuting predictions per model, $R = 100$; Section~\ref{sec:meth-null}), the conflict Moran's $I$ is significant ($p < 0.01$) in all 10 COMPAS runs and in all 10 German Credit runs.  This confirms that spatial clustering is not an artifact of the soft-variance metric but reflects genuine local concentration of hard-label disagreement.

\subsection{Synthetic Data Validation}
\label{sec:results-synthetic}

Experiments on synthetic datasets with known ground-truth ambiguous regions validate the pipeline.

\subsubsection{Single-Island Design}

A mixture of two well-separated Gaussian blobs (stable) and one ambiguous island (uniform disk with $P(y=1|x) \approx 0.5$) was constructed. The pipeline was run with the same model families and top-$K=25$ selection.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Moran's $I$:} 0.459 (highly significant; null mean $-0.001 \pm 0.008$, $p < 0.01$).
\item \textbf{HH recovery:} 101 HH points detected; precision = 0.99, recall = 0.91, Jaccard = 0.90 against the ground-truth island (110 points).
\item \textbf{FDR sensitivity:} Results are stable across $\alpha \in \{0.01, 0.05, 0.10, 0.20\}$; Jaccard ranges from 0.87 to 0.93.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Three-Islands Design}

A more challenging design with two stable blobs, three ambiguous islands, and isolated outliers was also tested.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{HH recovery:} At $\alpha = 0.05$, precision = 0.96, recall = 0.54, Jaccard = 0.52. Only 2 of 3 islands are captured as connected components.
\item \textbf{FDR sensitivity:} Recall improves with higher $\alpha$ (0.42 at $\alpha=0.01$ to 0.64 at $\alpha=0.20$), while precision remains above 0.94.
\end{itemize}

These results demonstrate that LISA HH classification reliably recovers designed ambiguous regions with high precision, and that recall scales with the FDR threshold. The single-island design achieves near-perfect recovery; the three-islands design shows that multiple, smaller ambiguous regions are partially recovered, with some islands potentially lacking sufficient local density for detection.

\subsection{Interpretable Rules for High-Multiplicity Regions}
\label{sec:results-rules}

To make hotspot findings actionable, we extract interpretable rules that describe HH and high-variance (HV, top 10\% by pointwise variance) regions in terms of original features.

\subsubsection{Rule Extraction Methods}

Four methods were applied: (1) shallow decision trees, (2) L1-regularized logistic regression, (3) cluster-describe (DBSCAN + rule fitting), and (4) beam search for conjunctive rules maximizing lift. Rules are evaluated by support (coverage), purity ($P(\text{label}=1 | \text{rule region})$), and lift (purity / base rate).

\subsubsection{Results on COMPAS}

On COMPAS (seed 0), the base rate for HV is 10.2\% (147 out of 1443 test points) and for HH is 3.3\% (48 points).

\begin{itemize}
\item Decision tree rules achieve purity up to 1.0 (lift $\approx 9.8$) for small rule regions (support $\sim$12).
\item L1 logistic regression identifies prior offence count ($+0.75$) and race (``Other'' category) as the most enriched features for HV regions.
\item Beam search finds HH rules with OOB purity $\sim$0.40 and lift $\sim$12.0; top features include prior offence count and age.
\item OOB bootstrap evaluation confirms rule stability: median purity remains within 10\% of in-sample estimates.
\end{itemize}

Figure~\ref{fig:pca_hv_hh} shows HV and HH points in PCA space for COMPAS, confirming that the identified regions occupy a coherent part of the feature space.

\subsection{Decision Boundary Proximity}
\label{sec:results-margin}

A natural hypothesis is that high-variance points are simply those near the decision boundary, where the ensemble mean prediction $\bar{p}_i$ is close to 0.5. We test this by computing the margin $m_i = |\bar{p}_i - 0.5|$ and correlating it with pointwise variance.

Table~\ref{tab:margin_summary} summarizes the results. On Breast Cancer and German Credit, variance and margin are \emph{negatively} correlated (Pearson $r = -0.85$ and $-0.56$ respectively), confirming that high-variance points tend to have low margin (near the boundary). However, on COMPAS the correlation is essentially zero ($r = -0.04 \pm 0.09$), indicating that multiplicity is \emph{not} simply a boundary phenomenon on this dataset.

\subsection{Summary of Results}

The experiments demonstrate that predictive multiplicity, as measured by both soft prediction variance and hard conflict ratio across the Rashomon set, is present in all three benchmark datasets and is spatially structured in feature space.  Key findings:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Spatial significance:} Global Moran's $I$ is positive and significant under the permutation null (Section~\ref{sec:meth-null}) for 90\% of runs overall (100\% for COMPAS and German, 70\% for Breast Cancer).  Conflict-based Moran's $I$ is significant in the same runs, confirming that spatial clustering extends to hard-label disagreement.

\item \textbf{Complementary disagreement metrics:} Soft variance and hard conflict identify largely different observations as problematic (Jaccard between variance-HH and conflict-HH $\approx 0.06$ on COMPAS).  Quadrant analysis shows that 7.7\% of COMPAS observations have high variance without label disagreement (quadrant B), and 8.7\% have label disagreement without high variance (quadrant C), demonstrating that both metrics are needed.

\item \textbf{Family differences:} Per-family analysis reveals that MLP and GBM produce the strongest spatial clustering, while LogReg shows the least multiplicity.  Model family explains 41--62\% of prediction variance; this share rises to 52--69\% inside HH hotspots.

\item \textbf{Hotspot stability:} HH regions occupy consistent regions of feature space across seeds (using a fixed test set), though the exact point sets vary (Jaccard $\sim$0.025).  About 6\% of COMPAS test points are HH in $\geq$50\% of runs---reflecting region-level persistence despite point-level variability.

\item \textbf{Robustness:} Findings are robust to $K \in \{5, \ldots, 50\}$ and $k \in \{10, \ldots, 50\}$.  Calibration does not remove spatial structure (Jaccard of HH pre/post calibration $\sim$0.42--0.78).

\item \textbf{Validation:} On synthetic data, LISA HH regions recover ground-truth ambiguous regions with precision 0.99 and recall 0.91.

\item \textbf{Interpretability:} HH regions can be described by compact rules with lift up to 12$\times$ the base rate, with key features (prior offence count, age) stable under bootstrap evaluation and consistent across seeds.

\item \textbf{Not purely a boundary effect:} On COMPAS, variance is essentially uncorrelated with decision-boundary margin ($r \approx -0.04$), showing that multiplicity reflects structural model disagreement rather than proximity to $\bar{p} = 0.5$.  On Breast Cancer and German, a moderate negative correlation exists.

\item \textbf{Fairness:} African-Americans on COMPAS have a higher HH rate ($\sim$7.4\%) than Caucasians ($\sim$2.9\%) and Hispanics ($\sim$1.9\%), though this disparity is not consistently significant across seeds ($p \approx 0.25$).

\item \textbf{Graph robustness:} Moran's $I$ and HH counts are nearly identical under Euclidean, PCA-reduced, and cosine-based kNN graphs, confirming that spatial findings are not artifacts of the distance metric.

\item \textbf{Aggregated drivers:} Family and HP importance rankings are highly stable across 10 seeds.  Model family explains 41--62\% of variance depending on dataset; LogReg regularization strength ($\rho > 0.93$) and kNN number of neighbors ($\rho > 0.70$) are the top within-family drivers across all datasets and seeds.
\end{enumerate}
