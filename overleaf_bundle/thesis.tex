\begin{abstract}
    
Modern machine learning pipelines often produce many models with near\hyp{}identical
predictive performance, yet these models can disagree substantially on individual
predictions, a phenomenon known as predictive multiplicity. Existing approaches quantify this disagreement globally or for individual observations,
but they do not directly analyze whether high-disagreement observations form coherent
regions in feature space or which modeling choices are associated with such regions.

This thesis introduces a spatial analysis framework for predictive multiplicity. We
compute observation-wise predictive variance across finite top-$K$ Rashomon
approximations selected by validation Brier score and analyze its structure using
spatial statistics, including global Moran's $I$ and Local Indicators of Spatial
Association (LISA). This enables the detection, localization, and statistical assessment
of multiplicity hotspots---regions in feature space where predictions are particularly
model-dependent. We further decompose predictive variance into between-family and
within-family hyperparameter components and analyze how these descriptive sources of
variation differ across the feature space.

Experiments on three benchmark datasets with five model families show that, under the
tested benchmark settings and the prediction-permutation null, predictive multiplicity is
often spatially structured. Model family accounts for a substantial share of observed
disagreement, with stronger hotspot-specific contributions on COMPAS and Adult. These
patterns remain visible under changes in Rashomon set size, neighborhood definition,
probability calibration, and graph construction. Synthetic experiments with known ground-truth instability regions validate the pipeline's ability
to recover both boundary-driven ambiguous regions and localized non-boundary exception regions.
High-multiplicity regions can additionally be characterized by compact, interpretable
rules in the original feature space.

The results suggest that predictive multiplicity is not merely a global property of model
classes, but can also appear as a localized phenomenon affecting specific regions of the
feature space. The proposed framework provides a diagnostic for identifying predictions
that are especially sensitive to the choice among near-optimal models, with potential implications for the reliability assessment and governance of automated
decision systems.
\end{abstract}

\section{Electronic appendix}
\label{el_app}

The electronic appendix contains the code, generated intermediate results, tables, and
figures used to reproduce the empirical analysis and thesis assets. The code repository is
available at:

\begin{center}
\url{https://github.com/DejvisT/rashomon-multiplicity}
\end{center}

The main repository is organized around the following entry points:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \path{run_training_pipeline.py}: trains candidate models, ranks them by
    validation Brier score, constructs the finite top-$K$ Rashomon approximations, and
    saves prediction matrices and run-level summaries.

    \item \path{run_training_pipeline_fixed_test.py}: runs the training pipeline with a held-out test set that is kept fixed across seeds for the hotspot stability analysis.

    \item \path{analysis/}: contains the main analysis modules, including multiplicity
    metrics, spatial analysis, calibration robustness, family and hyperparameter
    decomposition, and rule extraction.

    \item \path{notebooks/}: contains exploratory and analysis notebooks used during
    development. Final thesis-facing tables and figures are exported through the script
    listed below.

    \item \path{scripts/export_thesis_assets.py}: exports the LaTeX tables and PDF
    figures included in the thesis from the stored experiment outputs.

    \item \path{overleaf_bundle/presentation_assets/}: contains the generated tables
    and figures included by the LaTeX thesis.

    \item \path{requirements.txt}: records the Python package dependencies used for the
    experiments.
\end{itemize}

The experiments use Python with \texttt{scikit-learn} for model training and
\texttt{libpysal}/\texttt{esda} from PySAL for graph-based spatial statistics. Random seeds
are fixed throughout the pipeline to support reproducibility.
