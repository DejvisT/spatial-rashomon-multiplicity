\begin{abstract}

Modern machine learning pipelines often produce many models with near-identical predictive performance, yet these models can disagree substantially on individual predictions---a phenomenon known as \emph{predictive multiplicity}. Existing metrics quantify this disagreement globally but do not reveal \emph{where} in the feature space instability concentrates or \emph{what} drives it.

This thesis proposes a spatial analysis framework for predictive multiplicity. We compute observation-wise predictive variance across a Rashomon set of near-optimal models and apply spatial statistics---global Moran's $I$ and Local Indicators of Spatial Association (LISA)---to detect, localize, and test the significance of multiplicity hotspots in feature space. We decompose variance into between-family and within-family (hyperparameter) components and extract interpretable rules that describe high-multiplicity regions.

Experiments on three benchmark datasets (COMPAS, German Credit, Breast Cancer) with five model families, 250 candidate models, and 10 independent runs each show that: (i) predictive variance is spatially clustered (Moran's $I$ significant in 90\% of runs); (ii) model family explains 41--62\% of prediction variance, rising to 52--69\% inside hotspots; (iii) hotspot regions are robust to the Rashomon set size, neighborhood graph density, post-hoc calibration, and alternative distance metrics; and (iv) on COMPAS, hotspots are not reducible to decision-boundary proximity and can be described by compact rules involving age and prior criminal history. Validation on synthetic data with known ambiguous regions confirms that the pipeline recovers ground-truth instability with precision 0.99 and recall 0.91.

These findings demonstrate that predictive multiplicity is not merely a global nuisance but a spatially structured phenomenon that concentrates in identifiable subpopulations, with implications for the reliability, fairness, and interpretability of automated decision systems.

\end{abstract}
